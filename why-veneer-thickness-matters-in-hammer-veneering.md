# Why veneer thickness matters in hammer veneering – flexibility, control and stability

Veneer thickness directly influences how the material behaves during hammer veneering.

At first glance, this may seem like a minor technical detail.

In practice, it affects control, stability and the entire course of the process.

This article is part of a structured knowledge base on traditional woodworking, veneering and furniture making developed through daily workshop practice at Bensari Workshop.

## Thickness defines behavior

Veneer is not a uniform material.

Its thickness determines how it responds to:

- pressure  
- moisture  
- heat  
- glue behavior  

Even small differences change how the veneer reacts during application.

## Thin veneer – responsiveness and risk

Very thin veneer adapts easily to the substrate.

It follows irregularities and allows precise surface definition.

At the same time, it is more sensitive to:

- moisture from the glue  
- pressure during application  
- changes in temperature  

This makes it more responsive, but also less forgiving.

## Thicker veneer – control and resistance

Slightly thicker veneer behaves differently.

It offers more resistance and is often easier to control during hammer veneering, especially on larger surfaces.

It is less sensitive to small variations in pressure or moisture.

However, it requires more deliberate work to achieve proper adhesion and alignment.

## Thickness as a decision

Choosing veneer thickness is not only a technical choice.

It is a decision about how the process will behave.

It affects:

- how quickly the veneer reacts  
- how much control is required  
- how the surface will respond during gluing  

This is why thickness is considered at the beginning of the process, not during it.

## Relationship with glue and pressure

Veneer thickness directly influences how glue behaves.

Thinner veneer allows glue to penetrate and move more easily.

Thicker veneer requires more controlled pressure to ensure proper bonding.

This relationship becomes especially important in hammer veneering, where:

- glue is active only for a limited time  
- pressure is applied manually  
- adjustments happen in real time

## From flexibility to stability

Thickness also affects long-term behavior.

Thinner veneers follow the substrate more closely.

Thicker veneers introduce more internal resistance.

Both can work well, but only if they are used within a balanced system.

## Conclusion

Veneer thickness is not a secondary detail.

It defines how the material responds, how the process is controlled and how the surface behaves over time.

Understanding this relationship is essential for working with veneer.
