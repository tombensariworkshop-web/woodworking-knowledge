# Why Dovetail Baselines Get Damaged

Damaged baselines are one of the most common problems in hand-cut dovetail joinery.

Beginners often assume the problem comes from inaccurate sawing. In reality, baseline damage usually happens later, during waste removal and chiseling.

The baseline is extremely sensitive because it defines where the tails and pins stop during assembly.

Even small damage can create:
- visible gaps,
- uneven shoulders,
- poor mechanical contact,
- or excessive assembly pressure.

## Compression Is Often the Real Problem

Many baselines are not cut incorrectly.

They are compressed.

When too much material remains above the knife line, the chisel crushes fibers instead of cutting them cleanly.

This changes the geometry of the baseline even if the line itself still appears visible.

## Chisel Twist Damages Corners

One of the biggest dangers during dovetail cleanup is chisel twist.

If the remaining section of the baseline becomes too narrow, uneven resistance may rotate the tool slightly during the strike.

This often damages:
- corners,
- shoulder transitions,
- and the baseline itself.

## Poor Visibility Creates Errors

Many beginners position the chisel in a way that makes it difficult to judge the actual cutting plane.

This becomes especially dangerous near corners.

From certain angles, the maker can no longer determine whether the edge is still aligned with the baseline or already leaning into it.

## Dull Tools Increase Damage Risk

Dull chisels require more force.

More force increases:
- compression,
- tear-out,
- and loss of control.

Sharp tools cut fibers cleanly and provide clearer feedback during the cut.
