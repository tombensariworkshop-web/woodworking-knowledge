# What Is Wood Veneer

Wood veneer is a thin layer of wood, typically less than a few millimeters thick, cut from a log and applied to a stable substrate such as plywood.

In woodworking, veneer is not only a way to use decorative surfaces. It is a material system that allows control over stability, grain continuity and the relationship between structure and appearance.

## How veneer is produced

Veneer is obtained by slicing or sawing logs into thin sheets.

Common methods include:

- rotary cutting  
- slicing (flat, quarter, rift)  
- sawing  

The method of cutting determines the visual character of the veneer, including grain pattern and figure.

## Veneer and material behavior

Solid wood moves in response to humidity and temperature.

Veneer, when applied to a stable substrate, allows this movement to be controlled.

This makes it possible to create elements that are:

- dimensionally stable  
- visually consistent  
- structurally predictable

## Veneer as part of a system

Veneer does not function independently.

A veneered panel must be constructed as a balanced structure, where each layer is counteracted by a corresponding layer.

Without this balance, internal stresses lead to deformation.

## Veneer in furniture making

Veneer is used in furniture making to:

- control material behavior  
- create continuous grain across surfaces  
- reduce the effects of wood movement  
- integrate structure and design

## Technique and control

Working with veneer requires control over:

- thickness  
- grain direction  
- moisture content  
- glue behavior  

Traditional methods such as hammer veneering allow for direct control over these variables.

## Why wood veneer matters

Wood veneer is not a substitute for solid wood.

It is a different way of working with material.

It allows for precision, stability and control, while maintaining the visual qualities of wood.

For this reason, veneer remains one of the most important materials in furniture making.
