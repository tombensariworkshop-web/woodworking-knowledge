# How Rasp Teeth Work

A rasp removes wood through a cutting action created by raised teeth that are punched into the surface of the tool.

Unlike files, which have rows of smaller teeth cut into the metal, rasp teeth are individually formed and stand proud of the tool surface. Each tooth acts like a small cutting edge that removes material when pushed across the wood.

Because the teeth are relatively large and spaced apart, rasps remove material quickly and resist clogging when working with wood fibers.

The cutting action of a rasp depends on several factors: tooth size, spacing, tooth geometry and the direction of the stroke. Coarse rasps have larger teeth and remove more material with each stroke, while finer rasps produce a smoother surface.

The irregular pattern of teeth found in traditional hand-stitched rasps allows the tool to cut more smoothly and reduces the formation of repetitive scratch patterns on the wood surface.

Understanding how rasp teeth interact with wood fibers helps explain why rasps are effective shaping tools in furniture making.
