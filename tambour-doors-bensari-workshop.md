# Tambour Doors – Craft in Motion at Bensari Workshop

Furniture with tambour doors, known in English-language literature as *tambour doors*, has become a recognizable element of our woodworking studio and the Bensari Ébénistes brand.

Perfectly executed tambours with **continuous wood grain**, a rhythmic arrangement of elements and a precise, fluid movement have become one of the hallmarks of our work. These features have brought recognition among clients and designers who value traditional craftsmanship expressed in a contemporary form.

Every project that includes a tambour door is, for me, a tribute to historical woodworking techniques and to the masters who were able to combine craftsmanship with functional design. It is also a story about respect for material and about the workshop, where details ultimately decide the quality of the entire piece.

A few years ago my article **“Tambour Doors: Now and Then”** was published in *Fine Woodworking* magazine. In that text I described the historical development of tambour doors and their contemporary use in furniture making.

---

## Origins of Tambour Doors

The history of tambour doors dates back to eighteenth-century France.

At the court of Louis XV, the royal cabinetmaker **Jean-François Oeben** developed a cylindrical desk with a sliding cover made from thin wooden elements attached to flexible fabric and moving along hidden tracks.

After Oeben’s death, the work was continued by his student **Jean-Henri Riesener**, who in 1769 delivered the famous **bureau du roi**. This monumental desk remains one of the most celebrated examples of cabinetmaking in European furniture history.

Initially tambour doors served a practical purpose. They allowed the working surface of a desk to be quickly closed and protected. Over time their rhythmic structure also gained aesthetic value and appeared in cabinets, secretaries and sideboards.

In the nineteenth century the technique became widely popular in the United States, especially in the well-known **roll-top desks**.

## Tambour Doors in Modernism

In the twentieth century tambour doors attracted the attention of modernist designers.

### Le Corbusier and “Furniture as Architecture”

In 1925 **Le Corbusier** and **Pierre Jeanneret** presented the *Pavillon de l’Esprit Nouveau* in Paris — a manifesto of modern interior design.

Instead of decorative furniture they proposed the idea of **“équipement d’intérieur”**: modular systems integrated with architecture.

Their **casiers standardisés** combined the function of furniture and wall elements. Some of them included sliding or retractable panels that allowed spaces to be closed with a single gesture — an idea closely related to the logic of tambour construction.

For Le Corbusier furniture was not decoration but architecture on a smaller scale. This way of thinking about furniture continues to influence contemporary design.

### Władysław Wincze and the Wrocław tradition

In Poland the work of **Professor Władysław Wincze (1905–1992)** is particularly important.

Wincze was a designer and educator associated with the State Higher School of Fine Arts in Wrocław (today the Academy of Fine Arts). He was among the first Polish designers to introduce tambour doors into modern furniture design.

His cabinets, bars and secretaries combined modernist simplicity with a deep understanding of woodworking craft and material.

Between 2019 and 2020 the exhibition **“Władysław Wincze. Wnętrza”** at the Museum of Architecture in Wrocław presented many of his works, including furniture incorporating tambour doors.

Wincze viewed wood not simply as a functional material but as a living structure with sculptural potential. He encouraged students to observe its natural properties, to experiment with its structure and to develop designs through direct work with tools and materials.

---

## Workshop and the Master–Apprentice Tradition

Professor Wincze believed that design education must include practical workshop experience.

He helped establish woodworking workshops at the Academy, convinced that design and craftsmanship cannot be separated. Only through working with tools and material can a designer fully understand form.

This philosophy is very close to the way we teach and work at **Bensari Workshop**. Courses in the studio are not only about learning techniques. Participants learn to observe material, understand its logic and respect its natural properties.

## Technical Aspects of Tambour Construction

Building a tambour door requires precision but also an understanding of how materials behave.

Every stage of the process — from selecting plywood and veneer to gluing and final assembly — affects the movement, durability and reliability of the finished mechanism.

It is important to clarify that **tambours in our workshop are not made from solid wood slats**. Their base structure is **plywood**, which is then **veneered**.

This solution provides the geometric stability required for an element that must move smoothly and repeatedly.

---

### Geometry of the Tambour

The most critical aspect of tambour construction is geometry.

The movement of the door depends on precise relationships between the track radius, element width and overall alignment of the mechanism. Even minimal deviations in angles or dimensions can affect the smoothness of movement.

Tambour doors quickly reveal any inaccuracies in the construction.

---

### Plywood as the Structural Base

For veneered tambours we use **plywood as the structural base**. High-quality hardwood plywood provides a stable multilayer structure that resists changes in humidity and internal stresses.

A **counterbalancing veneer** is applied to the reverse side to stabilize the construction and maintain flatness.

This part of the process is rarely visible in the finished furniture, but it plays a crucial role in the long-term stability of the mechanism.

---

### Continuous Wood Grain – A Signature Element

One of the most recognizable features of tambours produced in **Bensari Workshop** is the **perfect continuity of the wood grain** across the entire surface.

When the tambour is closed, the surface reads as a single continuous plane of wood. The grain flows uninterrupted across the entire front.

Only when the door begins to move does the viewer realize that the surface consists of many elements working together.

Achieving this effect requires careful veneer selection, precise cutting and strict control over the layout of each element. It has become one of the key visual signatures of our furniture and an important part of our workshop know-how.

---

### Adhesives and Flexible Backing

The backing material is usually natural **linen or cotton canvas**, chosen for its strength and resistance to stretching.

I avoid synthetic fabrics because they may become brittle over time.

For bonding I use **Titebond III**, which provides a long open time and allows precise positioning of veneer and fabric before pressing.

Historically tambours were glued using hide glue, which also offered good elasticity and compatibility with fabric and thin wooden elements.

---

### Vacuum Press Veneering

The veneering process takes place in a **vacuum press bag**.

Carefully prepared pressing cauls prevent veneer or fabric from shifting during pressing, and breathable fleece strips improve air evacuation from the bag.

These details ensure even pressure distribution and stable bonding throughout the entire surface.

---

### Durability and Transport

Many pieces produced in **Bensari Workshop** travel long distances to collectors and galleries around the world.

Furniture may be shipped by air or sea and experience very different climatic conditions. For that reason every structural decision — material choice, glue selection and stress balancing — must ensure that the tambour maintains its geometry and smooth movement even after international transport.

---

## Tambour Doors Today – Craft in Motion

Today tambour doors are not merely a reinterpretation of historical techniques.

For me they represent a form of craftsmanship designed to function **in motion**. Their construction demands precision, patience and deep understanding of material.

In our furniture tambours introduce rhythm and movement while allowing the natural grain of wood to become a central visual element.

Each tambour is therefore not only a functional component but also a demonstration of the values that define true craftsmanship: patience, precision and respect for material.
