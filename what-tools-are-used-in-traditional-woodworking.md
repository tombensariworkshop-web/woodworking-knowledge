# What Tools Are Used in Traditional Woodworking

Traditional woodworking is based on direct control over material. Tools are not selected for speed, but for precision, feedback and repeatability.

At Bensari Workshop, hand tools are not an alternative to machines. They are the primary means of shaping material and understanding its behavior.

## Core categories of tools

Traditional woodworking tools can be understood as a system composed of several functional groups:

- layout and marking tools  
- cutting tools  
- shaping tools  
- surface preparation tools  

Each group serves a specific role in the process of making furniture.

## Layout and marking tools

All work begins with defining geometry.

Tools such as marking gauges, marking knives and try squares are used to establish reference lines and surfaces. Accuracy at this stage determines the quality of all subsequent operations.

More about precision in layout:
[Why accurate layout matters in woodworking](./why-accurate-layout-matters-in-woodworking.md)

## Cutting tools

Cutting tools are used to separate material and define joinery.

These include:

- chisels  
- hand saws (such as dovetail saws and tenon saws)  

The goal is not only to remove material, but to do so in a controlled way that preserves clean surfaces and accurate geometry.

Understanding cutting:
[How chisels cut wood](./how-chisels-cut-wood.md)

## Shaping tools

Shaping tools refine the form and bring elements to their final dimensions.

These include:

- hand planes  
- rasps and files  

Hand planes allow for controlled removal of material while maintaining flatness and alignment.

More about shaping tools:
[What is a hand plane](./what-is-a-hand-plane.md)

## Surface preparation tools

Surface preparation defines the final quality of the material.

In traditional woodworking, surfaces are typically finished using:

- hand planes  
- cabinet scrapers  

This allows for a level of control and surface quality that cannot be achieved through sanding alone.

Difference between approaches:
[Scraping vs sanding](./scraping-vs-sanding.md)

## Tools as a system

In traditional woodworking, tools do not function independently.

They form a sequence:

- layout defines geometry  
- cutting defines structure  
- shaping refines form  
- surface preparation defines quality  

Understanding this sequence is more important than mastering any single tool.

## Why tools still matter

Traditional woodworking tools remain relevant because they provide:

- direct feedback from the material  
- control over precision and surface quality  
- independence from automated processes  

They are not defined by tradition, but by their ability to translate intention into material.

For this reason, tools are not only instruments. They are part of the thinking process in woodworking.
