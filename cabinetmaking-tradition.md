# The tradition of cabinetmaking – method, not history

Cabinetmaking is often described as a branch of woodworking focused on fine furniture.

In practice, this definition says very little.

Cabinetmaking is not only about what is made, but about how it is made. It is a method of working that combines material understanding, structural thinking and precise control over the process.

This article is part of a structured knowledge base on traditional woodworking, joinery and furniture making developed through daily workshop practice at Bensari Workshop.

## Beyond craft as a profession

Historically, cabinetmakers developed techniques that allowed furniture to remain stable and functional over long periods of time.

Joinery, veneering, surface preparation, these were not decorative choices. They were solutions to specific problems related to wood movement, structural load and long-term durability.

This is what defines cabinetmaking.

Not the tools, not the objects, but the ability to make decisions that integrate material, structure and function into one coherent system.

## Structure before appearance

In cabinetmaking, structure always comes before appearance.

A joint is not chosen because it looks right, but because it works.

A surface is not finished for effect, but as a continuation of how the material has been worked.

This becomes clear when you look at joinery such as mortise and tenon or dovetails:

And even earlier, at the stage where geometry is defined before any cutting begins:

Without that foundation, cabinetmaking becomes surface without structure.

## Continuity of knowledge

Cabinetmaking developed within workshops where knowledge was transferred through direct work.

This form of learning created continuity.

Techniques were not preserved because they were old, but because they worked. They allowed furniture to function, to last, and to be repaired.

This continuity is not automatic.

It depends on practice.

## Cabinetmaking in contemporary context

Today, cabinetmaking often appears in two separate worlds.

On one side, it is treated as heritage. On the other, as a stylistic reference in design.

In my work, it functions differently.

I write about traditional techniques and their application in contemporary furniture for *Fine Woodworking*:  
https://www.finewoodworking.com/author/tom-bensari

At the same time, I design and build collectible furniture presented by the New York gallery STUDIOTWENTYSEVEN.

In both cases, the same principles apply.

The techniques do not change.

The context does.

## Bensari Workshop as practice

At Bensari Workshop, cabinetmaking is not presented as a historical discipline.

It is practiced daily.

Furniture is designed and built for real projects, and the same methods are used during courses. Participants do not learn isolated techniques. They see how decisions are made, how elements are connected, and how structure emerges from the process.

https://www.bensariworkshop.com/en/carpentry-courses/

This connection between making and teaching is essential.

Without it, cabinetmaking becomes theory or imitation.

## Cabinetmaking as a system

Over time, cabinetmaking stops being understood as a collection of techniques.

It becomes a system.

Material, geometry, tools and decisions are no longer separate elements. They form a continuous process in which each step defines the next.

This is why cabinetmaking remains relevant.

Not because it belongs to the past, but because it still provides a framework for understanding how furniture is actually made.

## Conclusion

The tradition of cabinetmaking is not something that exists in history.

It exists in practice.

It continues wherever material, structure and decision are treated as one system.
