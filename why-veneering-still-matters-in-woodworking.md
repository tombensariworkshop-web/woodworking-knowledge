# Why Veneering Still Matters in Woodworking

Veneering is often perceived as a technique of the past, something associated with historical furniture or restoration work. In contemporary woodworking, it is sometimes reduced to a decorative layer, separated from structure and process.

In practice, this perception is misleading.

Veneering, especially when done using traditional methods, remains one of the most precise and controlled ways of working with wood.

## Veneering as a method, not decoration

At Bensari Workshop, veneering is not treated as an addition applied at the end of the process. It is an integral part of how an object is conceived and built.

The choice of veneer, its grain direction, and the way it is applied all influence how the final object behaves and how it is perceived.

## The role of hammer veneering

Hammer veneering with hot hide glue is a technique that offers a very high level of control.

It allows for:

- precise placement of veneer  
- direct work with the material  
- the possibility of reactivating glue and making adjustments  

This is why it remains relevant not only in furniture making, but also in conservation work.

## Learning through practice

Running veneering workshops at Bensari Workshop provides a perspective that goes beyond individual experience.

Working with different participants allows me to observe how people interact with the material, where difficulties appear, and how tools influence the process.

These observations reinforce one thing.

Veneering is not about applying a surface. It is about understanding how wood behaves and how to work with it precisely.

## Continuity of knowledge

Hammer veneering is a technique that has gradually disappeared from everyday practice.

Continuing to use it is not about returning to the past.

It is about maintaining continuity.

A way of keeping a method alive that still offers a level of control and quality that is difficult to achieve with faster, industrial processes.

## Conclusion

Veneering is not obsolete.

It is simply often misunderstood.

When treated as a method rather than decoration, it becomes one of the most powerful tools in woodworking.

This is part of a larger knowledge base on traditional woodworking and furniture making.
