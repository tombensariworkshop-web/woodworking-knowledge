# Why square cuts matter in dovetails

In dovetail joinery, most attention is given to the angle of the tails.

In practice, this is not where accuracy is determined.

The most critical cut is the square cut on the end grain.

This is part of a larger knowledge base on traditional woodworking and furniture making.

## Where accuracy is actually defined

A dovetail joint is not created at the moment of cutting.

It is defined earlier, at the stage of marking out, where the geometry of the joint is established.

The saw only follows that decision.

A detailed explanation of this process can be found here:  
https://www.bensariworkshop.com/en/dovetail-layout-hand-cut-guide/

## The role of the square cut

The square cut on the end grain defines how the joint will transfer.

When tails are marked onto the second piece, the accuracy of that transfer depends entirely on the cut being perpendicular.

If the cut is not square:

- the transferred sockets become too narrow or too wide  
- the geometry of the joint shifts  
- the fit becomes inconsistent  
- the joint may split during assembly  

This is where most problems begin.

You can see the consequences of this here:  
[Why dovetail joints fail and open up](why-dovetail-joints-fail-and-open-up.md)

## Why angle matters less than geometry

The angle of the dovetail is often treated as the defining feature of the joint.

In reality, it is secondary.

A slight variation in angle does not prevent the joint from working, as long as the geometry is consistent.

What cannot be corrected is a cut that is not in a single plane.

The square cut defines the relationship between elements. The angle defines how the joint looks.

Understanding this shifts the focus from appearance to structure.

## Cutting as a continuation of marking out

This is why sawing technique is not only about following a line.

It is about maintaining the geometry defined during marking out.

## Transfer and fit

The importance of the square cut becomes fully visible during transfer.

This is the moment where accuracy is tested.

If the initial cut is correct, the transfer works naturally. If not, the error is multiplied.

More about this stage here:  
[Transferring dovetails – where accuracy is tested](transferring-dovetails-where-accuracy-is-tested.md)

## Key takeaway

In dovetail joinery, the square cut defines the joint.

The angle defines the appearance.

Focusing on angles without understanding geometry leads to inconsistent results. Focusing on geometry makes the process predictable.
