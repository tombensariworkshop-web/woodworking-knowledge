# Why square cuts matter in dovetails

In dovetail joinery, the most critical cut is not the angled side of the tail, but the square cut on the end grain.

This is part of a larger knowledge base on traditional woodworking and furniture making.

For context, see [how to saw dovetail tails correctly](./how-to-saw-dovetail-tails-correctly.md) and [transferring dovetails is where accuracy is tested](./transferring-dovetails-where-accuracy-is-tested.md).

## What happens if the cut is not square?

If the cut on the end grain is not perpendicular:

- the transferred sockets will be too narrow or too wide  
- the joint may split during assembly  
- gaps may appear  
- the geometry of the joint will be incorrect  

See [why dovetail joints fail and open up](./why-dovetail-joints-fail-and-open-up.md).

## Why is this more important than the dovetail angle?

The dovetail angle can vary slightly without affecting the function of the joint.

But the square cut defines how the joint transfers and fits.

This is why geometry matters more than appearance.

See [dovetail joint](./dovetail-joint.md) and [what is a dovetail joint](./what-is-a-dovetail-joint.md).

## Can a dovetail still work with an imperfect angle?

Yes.

If the geometry is consistent, even an imperfect angle will transfer correctly and produce a working joint.

The problem begins when the cut is not in a single plane.

## Key takeaway

In dovetails, the square cut defines the joint.

The angle defines the appearance.
