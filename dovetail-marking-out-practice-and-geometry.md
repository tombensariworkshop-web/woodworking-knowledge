# Dovetail Marking Out – Practice, Geometry, and Control

When people struggle with dovetails, they usually look at the saw. In my experience, that is the wrong place to start.

This article is part of a structured knowledge base on traditional woodworking, joinery, and furniture making developed through daily workshop practice at Bensari Workshop.

The real problems appear earlier, when I define the geometry of the joint through marking out.

## Marking out is where the joint begins

Working with hand tools every day, I treat marking out as the stage where the joint is already decided.

At this point I define proportions, alignment and behaviour of the joint. Cutting only follows.

## Establishing the baseline

The baseline defines the depth of the joint.

I transfer it from the thickness of the mating piece using a square and working from the reference edge. I rely heavily on tactile feedback, checking contact with my fingers, not just visually.

## Dividing the tails

There is no universal rule here.

I work with proportion and function. Hidden joints tend to be more regular, visible ones allow more variation.

The method matters less than control.
