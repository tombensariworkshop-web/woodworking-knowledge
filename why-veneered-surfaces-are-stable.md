# Why Veneered Surfaces Are Stable

Veneered panels are often more stable than comparable surfaces made from solid wood.

This stability comes from the way veneer layers interact with the substrate beneath them.

## Thin Decorative Layer

Veneer is typically very thin compared with solid boards. Because the decorative layer is so thin, it cannot move independently to the same degree as solid wood.

Instead, the veneer is constrained by the substrate to which it is glued.

## Stable Substrates

In many furniture constructions veneer is applied to stable substrates such as panel materials or carefully constructed wooden cores.

These substrates limit dimensional movement across the surface.

By combining a thin decorative veneer with a stable substrate, cabinetmakers can create wide surfaces that remain relatively flat even when humidity changes.

## Balanced Construction

Traditional veneered panels often use balanced construction, meaning veneer is applied to both sides of a panel.

This helps equalize stresses created by changes in moisture and reduces the likelihood of warping.

## Historical Practice

This structural principle explains why veneering has been widely used in furniture making for centuries.

Large table tops, cabinet doors and decorative panels were often veneered to achieve both visual richness and structural stability.
