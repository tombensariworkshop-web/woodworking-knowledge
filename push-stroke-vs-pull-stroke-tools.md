# Push Stroke vs Pull Stroke Tools

Woodworking tools can be designed to cut either on the push stroke or on the pull stroke. This difference reflects two major traditions of tool design found in woodworking cultures around the world.

Understanding how these systems work helps explain differences between Western and Japanese woodworking tools.

## Push Stroke Tools

Most traditional Western woodworking tools are designed to cut on the push stroke.

Planes, saws and many shaping tools are pushed forward while cutting. This design often relies on the weight of the tool and the forward motion of the user's body to drive the cut.

Push stroke tools tend to be heavier and rely on mass and stability to maintain momentum during the cut.

## Pull Stroke Tools

Many Japanese woodworking tools are designed to cut on the pull stroke.

Pull saws and Japanese planes are pulled toward the user rather than pushed away. Because the blade is placed in tension during the cut, these tools can often be thinner and lighter.

This approach can provide excellent control and precision in certain tasks.

## Differences in Control

Both systems are effective and capable of producing precise work.

The choice between push stroke and pull stroke tools often depends on tradition, tool design and the preferences of the woodworker.

Regardless of the cutting direction, both approaches rely on sharp tools and a clear understanding of wood behavior.
