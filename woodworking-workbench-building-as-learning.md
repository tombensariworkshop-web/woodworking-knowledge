# Building a Workbench as a Way of Learning Woodworking

For a long time, I treated the workbench as something obvious.

It was simply there, a necessary element of the workshop, something I used without questioning how it was made or why it worked the way it did. My attention was focused on tools, techniques, and projects, while the bench remained in the background.

That changed when I built my own.

## From using a bench to understanding it

There is a clear difference between working on a bench and building one.

When you build it yourself, every decision becomes visible. You begin to understand why mass matters, how joinery transfers force, and why proportions are not arbitrary. The relationship between legs, top, and structure stops being abstract and becomes something you can feel directly in the material.

This is something I only fully understood through practice, because the bench gradually reveals itself as a system that supports everything else.

## Roubo and the logic of the design

The benches we build at Bensari Workshop are based on Benchcrafted plans, which draw directly from the work of André Jacob Roubo, an 18th-century French cabinetmaker and author.

Roubo described his workbench in *L’Art du Menuisier* as a practical solution developed for hand tool work, where mass, simplicity, and structural clarity follow directly from experience rather than theory.

What is striking is how relevant this design remains today.

Contemporary interpretations, such as the split top workbench built with Benchcrafted hardware, extend this idea by introducing new workholding possibilities, while preserving the fundamental principle of stability and direct support for hand tool work.

## Building benches as part of real practice

Working with apprentices from Les Compagnons du Devoir brought another layer of understanding to this process.

As part of the Erasmus+ programme, they spent several weeks at Bensari Workshop in Wrocław, working intensively with hand tools and traditional joinery.

Each day followed a clear structure based on real tasks. They worked with planes, developed precision in marking, and built joints such as mortise and tenon and dovetails, moving gradually from individual elements to full constructions.

## Building Roubo benches in the workshop

The culmination of their stay was the construction of three Roubo workbenches.

These were full split-top benches made from steamed beech, built according to Benchcrafted plans and hardware. We worked with approximately one cubic metre of material, moving through the entire process from selecting timber to final assembly.

Preparing elements, gluing, planing, cutting traditional joints, and assembling the structure required sustained attention and consistency at every stage.

Through this process, the bench reveals itself as a demanding construction that requires a clear understanding of material, proportion, and force.

## Why building a workbench is the best starting point

If I were to recommend one project to someone who wants to begin woodworking in a conscious way, it would be building a workbench.

It contains all the essential elements of the craft.

Joinery becomes structural rather than theoretical. Mortise and tenon joints determine stability over time. Wood movement has to be anticipated and managed. Proportions become functional decisions.

At the same time, the process requires patience and consistency, because each stage builds directly on the previous one.

## Conclusion

Building a workbench changes the way woodworking is understood, because it brings together material, structure, and process into a single, coherent experience.

Working with the apprentices made this particularly clear, as the benches they built now carry a record of the work, discipline, and decisions that shaped them.

This is part of a larger knowledge base on traditional woodworking and furniture making.
