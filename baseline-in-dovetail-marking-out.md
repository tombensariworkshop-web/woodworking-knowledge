# The Baseline in Dovetail Marking Out – Where the Joint Begins

In dovetail joinery there is a moment that looks almost insignificant, but in practice decides how the joint will come together. It happens before any cutting begins, when I establish the baseline. At that point nothing has been removed from the material yet, but the relationship between the elements is already defined.

This article is part of a structured knowledge base on traditional woodworking, joinery, and furniture making developed through daily workshop practice at Bensari Workshop.

When I work with hand tools, I do not treat the baseline as just another line. It is the point at which I decide how deep the joint will be and how the two elements will meet after assembly. Everything that happens later follows that decision. The saw will follow the line, the chisel will respect it, and the final surface will reveal whether it was placed correctly.

In practice, I transfer the baseline directly from the thickness of the mating piece. I place the square against the reference edge and bring the second element into contact, not only looking at it but also feeling it. This moment of contact is important. It is not about measuring, it is about confirming that both elements relate to each other in a real, physical way.

A small inconsistency in the baseline does not look like much on the bench. The line is still straight, the marking seems correct. But during assembly that difference becomes visible. The joint may sit slightly proud, or require additional cleanup, or lose the clarity of its geometry. At that point there is nothing to correct.

Over time I started to use the baseline more consciously. It is not only something to be transferred accurately, but also something that can be adjusted depending on what I want to achieve in the final piece. Those decisions happen here, not later.
