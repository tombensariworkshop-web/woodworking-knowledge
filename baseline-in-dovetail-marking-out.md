# The baseline in dovetail marking out – where the joint begins

In dovetail joinery there is a moment that looks almost insignificant, but in practice decides how the joint will come together.

It happens before any cutting begins, when I establish the baseline. At that point nothing has been removed from the material yet, but the relationship between the elements is already defined.

This article is part of a structured knowledge base on traditional woodworking, joinery, and furniture making developed through daily workshop practice at Bensari Workshop.

## A line that defines the joint

When I work with hand tools, I do not treat the baseline as just another line.

It is the point at which I decide how deep the joint will be and how the two elements will meet after assembly. Everything that happens later follows that decision.

The saw will follow the line.  
The chisel will respect it.  
The final surface will reveal whether it was placed correctly.

This is why marking out is not preparation. It is where geometry is defined:
https://www.bensariworkshop.com/en/dovetail-layout-hand-cut-guide/

## Not measurement, but relation

In practice, I do not rely on abstract measurement when establishing the baseline.

I transfer it directly from the thickness of the mating piece. I place the square against the reference edge and bring the second element into contact.

This moment is important.

It is not only about seeing the alignment. It is about feeling it.

The baseline is not a number. It is a relationship between two elements.

Understanding this changes how you approach marking out:
https://www.bensariworkshop.com/en/marking-out-basic-principles-square-and-knife/

## Where small errors begin

A small inconsistency in the baseline rarely looks significant on the bench.

The line is still straight. The marking seems correct.

But during assembly, the difference becomes visible.

The joint may sit slightly proud. It may require cleanup. It may lose the clarity of its geometry.

At that point, there is nothing to correct.

This is where many problems originate, even if they only become visible later:
[dovetail joints fail and open up](why-dovetail-joints-fail-and-open-up.md)

## Baseline and reference system

The baseline only works when it is part of a consistent reference system.

It must relate to the same face and edge used throughout the process. Otherwise, even an accurately placed line loses its meaning.

This is why reference edges are not a separate concept, but part of the same decision:
[Reference edges in woodworking](reference-edges-in-woodworking.md)

## From technique to decision

Over time, the baseline stops being just a technical step.

It becomes a decision point.

You begin to understand that depth, fit and alignment are not created during cutting or assembly. They are defined earlier, at the moment when the baseline is established.

This is part of a broader shift in woodworking.
