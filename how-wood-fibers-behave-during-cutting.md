# How Wood Fibers Behave During Cutting

Wood is composed of long cellular fibers that run primarily along the grain of the tree. When woodworking tools interact with wood, they do not simply remove material — they interact with this fiber structure.

Understanding how wood fibers behave during cutting helps explain why grain direction matters, why tear-out occurs and why sharp cutting tools produce cleaner surfaces.

## The Structure of Wood Fibers

Wood is made of elongated cells that form bundles running along the grain. These fibers give wood its strength and determine how the material reacts when it is cut.

When a cutting tool moves across the surface, it encounters these fibers and separates them from the surrounding structure. The way the fibers separate depends on several factors:

- grain direction  
- sharpness of the tool  
- cutting angle  
- thickness of the shaving

Because wood is anisotropic, meaning its properties differ depending on direction, cutting across or against the grain produces different results.

## Cutting With the Grain

When a tool cuts with the grain, the blade slices the fibers in a direction that supports a clean separation.

The fibers bend slightly ahead of the blade and are then severed as the cutting edge passes through them. This produces a continuous shaving and a smooth surface.

Tools such as hand planes, chisels and spokeshaves rely on this slicing action to produce clean results.

## Cutting Against the Grain

When cutting against the grain, the blade may lift fibers ahead of the cutting edge instead of slicing them cleanly.

If the fibers split below the cutting line, the surface can tear away in small fragments. This is known as tear-out.

Tear-out is most common when grain direction changes or when working wood with irregular grain patterns.

## Controlling Fiber Separation

Traditional woodworking tools are designed to control how fibers separate during cutting.

In hand planes, several elements help manage this process:

- the cutting angle of the blade  
- the position of the chipbreaker  
- the mouth opening of the plane

These features work together to limit how far fibers can lift ahead of the blade, reducing the risk of tear-out.

## Fiber Behavior and Surface Quality

The behavior of wood fibers during cutting has a direct effect on surface quality.

When fibers are sliced cleanly, the resulting surface is smooth and reflective. When fibers are crushed, torn or abraded, the surface becomes rougher and scatters light differently.

This is one of the reasons why surfaces prepared with cutting tools often look different from surfaces prepared with sandpaper.

## Understanding Wood in Craft Practice

Developing skill in woodworking involves learning to recognize how wood fibers behave during cutting.

Experienced woodworkers observe grain direction, adjust their tools and choose cutting strategies that work with the material rather than against it.

At Bensari Workshop, participants learn how grain direction influences tool behavior and surface quality. Through practical work with planes, chisels and shaping tools they observe how wood fibers respond to different cutting conditions.

This understanding forms part of the deeper material knowledge that underlies traditional cabinetmaking.
