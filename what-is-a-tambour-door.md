# What is a tambour door?

A tambour door (often called a roll-top or sliding tambour) is a flexible door made of many narrow wooden slats connected on the back with fabric or canvas. The slats move together along curved tracks, allowing the door to slide or roll into a cabinet.

In practice it behaves differently from a hinged door. Instead of opening outward, the door disappears into the furniture itself, moving along a track inside the carcass.

Tambour doors are often associated with roll-top desks from the late 19th and early 20th centuries, but the idea appears in furniture much earlier and is still used today in contemporary cabinetry.

---

## How a tambour door actually works

A tambour door is built from many thin wooden strips. Each strip is glued onto a flexible backing, traditionally canvas or fabric. When assembled, the slats create a continuous wooden surface that can bend and travel through a curved track.

The movement of the door depends on three elements working together:

- narrow wooden slats  
- flexible backing material  
- precisely shaped tracks inside the cabinet  

If the tracks are not accurate, the tambour will immediately reveal it. Even a small misalignment can cause the door to bind or stall halfway through the curve.

This is why tambour construction is often described in books as a technique — but in practice it is really a matter of precision.

---

## Lessons from the workbench

Tambour doors look elegant and simple when finished, but they are rarely simple to make.

In the workshop the most time is usually spent not on the slats themselves but on preparation. The strips must be consistent in width and thickness, and the edges need to be carefully eased so that the slats move smoothly against one another.

Another important step is the track.

When building tambours in the workshop we often test the track several times before attaching the slats. A door that moves perfectly on the bench can behave very differently once it enters the cabinet.

A fraction of a millimeter matters.

---

## Historical furniture and modern use

Tambour doors became widely known through roll-top desks produced in the late nineteenth century. These desks used long tambour curtains that disappeared into curved cavities inside the desk body.

The same principle appears in many other types of furniture:

- cabinets  
- sideboards  
- media consoles  
- wall storage systems  

Today tambours are often used in contemporary furniture where designers want to avoid the visual weight of hinged doors.

The door can slide away without occupying space around the cabinet, which makes it particularly useful in compact interiors.

---

## Tambour doors today

In contemporary furniture making the technique has evolved. Makers experiment with different materials, wider slats, new adhesives and CNC-cut tracks.

Despite these changes, the core principle remains the same as in historical furniture: many small pieces working together as one flexible surface.

For woodworkers the tambour door is an interesting example of how furniture construction combines engineering, material knowledge and patience at the workbench.
