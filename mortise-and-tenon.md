# Mortise and tenon joinery

Mortise and tenon joinery is one of the most fundamental structural systems in furniture making. It is widely used not because it is traditional, but because it solves a specific problem. It creates a stable connection that can carry load while maintaining alignment over time.

In practice, this joint is not defined by its shape alone. Its quality depends on how it is laid out, how it is cut, and how it relates to the rest of the structure.

## More than a connection

The joint consists of two elements. A tenon formed on one piece and a mortise cut into another.

When fitted correctly, the joint provides both strength and precision. The shoulders of the tenon register against the surface of the mortised element, which defines alignment and controls geometry.

This is why mortise and tenon is not only about strength. It is about structure.

## Where the joint actually begins

Like all joinery, mortise and tenon does not begin with cutting.

It begins with marking out.

The position of the mortise, the thickness of the tenon, and their relationship to reference faces are all defined before any material is removed. If this stage is unclear, the joint will never be accurate.

A clear example of how layout determines the final result can be seen here:  
https://www.bensariworkshop.com/en/dovetail-layout-hand-cut-guide/

And although this example is based on dovetails, the same principles apply directly to mortise and tenon construction.

## Cutting and control

Once the geometry is defined, the process becomes a matter of execution.

The mortise is cut, typically with chisels, in a way that preserves alignment and avoids widening the cavity. The tenon is then formed to match the mortise precisely.

This stage reveals whether the earlier decisions were correct.

If the marking out was accurate, the joint fits naturally. If not, the process becomes corrective, and correction in woodworking always comes at a cost.

Understanding how tools work in this context is essential:  
https://www.bensariworkshop.com/en/hand-tools-for-mortise-and-tenon-joinery-what-you-need-and-how-to-use-it/

## Structure and material behavior

Mortise and tenon joints are often used in frames where elements are subjected to load and movement.

Wood expands and contracts depending on moisture. The joint must be designed to work with that movement, not against it.

This is why proportions, fit, and orientation matter as much as the cutting itself.

## Mortise and tenon as a system

This joint connects multiple aspects of woodworking:

- marking out and reference surfaces  
- tool control  
- material understanding  
- structural thinking  

It cannot be understood as an isolated technique.
