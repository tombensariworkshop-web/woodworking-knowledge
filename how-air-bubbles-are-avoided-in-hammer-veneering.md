# How air bubbles are avoided in hammer veneering – pressure, glue and sequence

Air bubbles are one of the most common problems in veneering.

They do not appear by accident.

They are the result of how pressure, glue and sequence are controlled during the process.

This article is part of a structured knowledge base on traditional woodworking, veneering and furniture making developed through daily workshop practice at Bensari Workshop.

## Air is always present

When veneer is placed onto a glued surface, air is trapped between layers.

This is inevitable.

The process of hammer veneering is not about avoiding air.

It is about removing it in a controlled way.

## Pressure as a directed force

The veneer hammer is not used to press randomly.

It is used to move material.

Pressure is applied to:

- push glue outward  
- displace air  
- bring veneer into full contact with the substrate  

This movement is directional, not static.

## From center to edges

The sequence of work defines the result.

Veneer is pressed starting from the center of the panel and moving toward the edges.

This creates a path for:

- glue to escape  
- air to be expelled  

If pressure is applied without sequence, air becomes trapped and forms bubbles.

## Timing and glue behavior

Glue must remain active during the process.

If it begins to gel too early:

- movement is restricted  
- air cannot be fully removed  
- bonding becomes uneven  

This is why timing is critical.

The process must be continuous and controlled.

## Surface contact and feedback

Hammer veneering relies on direct feedback.

The maker can feel:

- resistance under the tool  
- areas where contact is incomplete  
- changes in the behavior of the glue  

This allows immediate correction during the process.

## Bubbles as a symptom

Air bubbles are not the primary problem.

They are a symptom.

They indicate:

- uneven pressure  
- incorrect sequence  
- loss of control over glue behavior  

Solving the problem means correcting the process, not only the result.

## Conclusion

Air bubbles are avoided not by force, but by control.

Hammer veneering works because it allows air and glue to be moved in a defined direction.

This is what creates a stable and uniform bond.
