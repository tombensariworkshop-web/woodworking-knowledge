# Hand Planes in Woodworking — Control and Surface

Hand planes are often reduced to finishing tools, something you reach for at the very end of the process to make a surface look better. In practice, they play a much more fundamental role.

At Bensari Workshop, planes are used from the very beginning of the work. They are not only about refinement, but about establishing flatness, straightness, and geometry. In other words, they define how the object will behave long before it starts to resemble a finished piece of furniture.

## Where planes fit in the system

Woodworking tools do not function as isolated objects. They form a sequence of decisions and actions, where each step depends on the previous one.

That sequence begins with marking, where geometry is defined:
https://www.bensariworkshop.com/en/marking-out-basic-principles-square-and-knife/

It continues with cutting, usually with a saw, and only then do planes come into play — not to decorate, but to bring surfaces into alignment with the intended geometry.

If you are starting your journey with hand tools, I described the broader context here:
https://www.bensariworkshop.com/en/basic-woodworking-tools-where-to-begin/

## Surface is a result, not a goal

A planed surface is often associated with aesthetics, but what you actually see is a record of decisions.

It reflects how well the tool is sharpened, how the grain was read, and how pressure was applied. It is not an effect added at the end, but a direct consequence of control throughout the process.

## Relation to marking

One of the most important things to understand is that a plane cannot correct a mistake in geometry.

If the marking was inaccurate, planing will only make that inaccuracy cleaner and more visible. This is why marking remains the foundation of all further work:
https://www.bensariworkshop.com/en/square-marking-shoulders-in-woodworking/
