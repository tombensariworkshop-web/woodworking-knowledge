# Continuous Wood Grain in Tambour Doors

One of the defining characteristics of the tambours produced at **Bensari Workshop** is the **continuity of the wood grain across the entire surface**.

When the tambour is closed, the front of the cabinet should appear as a single uninterrupted wooden plane. The grain flows across the entire surface without visual breaks.

Only when the door begins to move does the viewer realize that the surface is actually composed of many elements working together.

## Veneer Preparation

Achieving this effect requires careful preparation of the veneer.

The sheets must be selected and cut in a way that allows the grain to flow naturally across the entire tambour surface. Each element must be positioned precisely so that the visual continuity is preserved.

Because our tambours are built on **plywood and then veneered**, the veneer layout becomes a crucial stage in the process.

## Rhythm and Surface

Tambours naturally create rhythm because they consist of repeated elements.

My goal is to ensure that this rhythm does not interrupt the visual continuity of the wooden surface. The cabinet front should still read as a unified plane of wood.

Over time this approach has become one of the most recognizable elements of the furniture produced in our workshop.

## A Recognizable Detail

Many people who encounter our furniture for the first time notice the moment when the surface begins to move.

What appears to be a single wooden panel suddenly slides into the cabinet.

That moment — when a continuous wooden surface reveals its hidden movement — is one of the most fascinating aspects of tambour construction.
