# When should you stop learning woodworking online and start in a workshop?

At the beginning, learning woodworking online makes sense.

You explore.  
You observe.  
You try to understand the basics.

I did the same.

I spent years learning from books, videos, and online resources. I travelled across Europe searching for knowledge and trying to understand how traditional woodworking really works.

But at some point, something became clear.

There is a moment when online learning stops moving you forward.

## The moment when progress slows down

You recognize this moment when:

- your joints do not fit as they should  
- your surfaces are inconsistent  
- your results vary from attempt to attempt  

You understand what should happen.

But you cannot consistently make it happen.

This is not a lack of effort.

It is a lack of **direct feedback**.

## Why this happens

Woodworking is based on control.

Control of:
- tool  
- material  
- movement  

These cannot be refined through observation alone.

Without correction, you repeat the same mistakes — sometimes for months or years.

And because the process feels familiar, it is easy to believe you are improving.

In reality, you are stabilizing error.

## What changes in a real workshop

The moment you start working with an experienced craftsman, everything accelerates.

Not because you learn more information.

But because:
- your mistakes are corrected immediately  
- your technique is adjusted in real time  
- your understanding becomes physical, not abstract  

A small correction in how you hold a tool can change everything.

## My own turning point

For me, this shift happened when I moved from learning alone to learning through direct contact.

That is when woodworking started to make sense.

That experience shaped how I built Bensari Workshop.

Today, it is a place where:
- furniture is designed and built daily  
- education happens inside real work  
- participants learn through doing, not watching  

## When should you make the transition?

If you feel that:
- you understand more than you can execute  
- your results are inconsistent  
- your progress has slowed  

it is time to move from online learning to a real workshop.
