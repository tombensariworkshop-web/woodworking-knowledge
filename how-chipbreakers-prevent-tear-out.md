# How Chipbreakers Prevent Tear-Out

A chipbreaker is a small metal component mounted on the back of a plane iron in traditional bench planes. Although it appears simple, the chipbreaker plays an important role in controlling how wood fibers separate during the cutting process.

Understanding how chipbreakers work helps explain how cabinetmakers manage difficult grain and reduce tear-out when using hand planes.

## What Is a Chipbreaker

In a bevel-down hand plane, the cutting iron is paired with a secondary piece of steel known as the chipbreaker (sometimes called the cap iron). The chipbreaker sits directly above the cutting edge and bends the shaving as it rises from the surface of the wood.

This bending action changes the way the shaving forms and helps control how wood fibers lift ahead of the blade.

## What Causes Tear-Out

Tear-out occurs when the blade lifts wood fibers ahead of the cut instead of slicing them cleanly.

Wood fibers do not always run parallel to the surface. When grain direction changes, the blade can pull fibers upward before they are fully cut. This produces rough areas where the surface breaks below the cutting line.

Several factors influence tear-out:

- grain direction  
- cutting angle  
- blade sharpness  
- plane setup

## How the Chipbreaker Changes the Cut

When the chipbreaker is set close to the cutting edge, it forces the shaving to bend sharply as it leaves the blade.

This bending action prevents the shaving from lifting fibers too far ahead of the cutting edge. Instead, the fibers are forced to break closer to the blade, which reduces the likelihood of tear-out.

A properly set chipbreaker therefore changes the mechanics of the cut. Instead of allowing fibers to split ahead of the blade, it forces the wood to shear at the cutting edge.

## Chipbreaker Position

The distance between the cutting edge and the chipbreaker greatly affects performance.

For general planing tasks, the chipbreaker may be set slightly behind the cutting edge. When working difficult grain, woodworkers often move the chipbreaker much closer to the edge to increase its effect.

However, if the chipbreaker is placed too close to the cutting edge, it may make the plane harder to push or cause shavings to clog.

Proper setup therefore depends on the wood being planed and the thickness of the shaving.

## Chipbreakers and Plane Geometry

The chipbreaker works together with other elements of plane design, including the cutting angle and mouth opening of the plane.

A tight mouth supports the fibers directly ahead of the blade, while the chipbreaker bends the shaving and helps control the separation of fibers.

Together these elements allow traditional bench planes to work effectively even in challenging grain conditions.

## Chipbreakers in Workshop Practice

Although bevel-up planes are popular for certain tasks, the chipbreaker remains one of the defining features of traditional bevel-down bench planes.

In cabinetmaking workshops, properly setting the chipbreaker is considered an essential skill. It allows woodworkers to manage tear-out and produce clean surfaces even when working difficult wood.

At Bensari Workshop, participants in woodworking courses learn how plane setup, blade sharpness and chipbreaker position affect the behavior of the tool. Through practical work they observe how small adjustments influence the interaction between blade geometry and wood fibers.

Understanding this relationship is part of the broader knowledge that connects traditional hand tools with contemporary furniture making practice.
