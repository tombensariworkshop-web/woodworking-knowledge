# Can you learn woodworking online, or do you need a real workshop?

This is one of the most common questions today, and I understand where it comes from. Access to knowledge has never been easier. You can watch hundreds of videos, read books, follow structured online courses, and at first it seems that this should be enough to learn woodworking.

At the beginning of my own path, I did exactly that. I was looking for knowledge wherever I could find it. I learned from books on traditional woodworking, I followed online courses, and I travelled across Europe trying to find places where this craft was still practiced seriously. What I was missing was not information. What I was missing was direct contact with people who actually worked with wood every day.

This difference is fundamental.

Woodworking is not only about understanding what should be done. It is about how the work is carried out in practice. How you hold a tool, how you position your body, how you read the grain, how you react when something starts to go wrong. These are physical decisions made in real time, and they determine the outcome of every project.

In psychology, this is described as social learning. We acquire skills through observation, imitation, and interaction with other people. This process allows knowledge to become embodied. Without it, learning remains partial. You may understand the idea, but you do not develop the ability to execute it consistently.

This is one of the main limitations of online learning.

The problem is not that you cannot learn anything this way. The problem is that you may learn incorrectly, and without feedback you will not know it. A small inaccuracy in marking out, a subtle mistake in tool control, or a misunderstanding of grain direction can become a habit. In woodworking, every step builds on the previous one, so these small errors accumulate and begin to affect joinery, geometry, and ultimately the quality of the object.

An experienced craftsman is able to see these mistakes immediately and correct them before they become part of your process. This kind of correction cannot be replaced by a video or a written instruction.

This is precisely why I created Bensari Workshop in the form it has today. It is a working furniture atelier where design and making happen every day, and education is part of this process. Furniture is built here for interior architects and designers, and the knowledge shared during courses comes directly from that practice, from working with material, making structural decisions, and taking responsibility for the final result.

Participants do not learn isolated techniques. They observe and take part in a complete process, from the first layout lines to the final detail. They see how traditional techniques such as mortise and tenon, dovetail joinery, or hammer veneering function in real work, not as demonstrations but as tools used to solve actual problems.

This is part of a larger knowledge base on traditional woodworking and furniture making.

You can begin learning woodworking online. But if you want to develop real skill, precision, and understanding, at some point you need to move into a real workshop and work alongside someone who practices the craft every day.
