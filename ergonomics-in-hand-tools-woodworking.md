# Ergonomics in Hand Tools — How Tools Shape the Way We Work

For a long time, I understood ergonomics in woodworking as something related mainly to comfort. It seemed like a secondary aspect of tools, something that might make work more pleasant, but not necessarily more precise.

That perception changed gradually, and largely through teaching.

Running woodworking courses at Bensari Workshop creates a situation that is difficult to reproduce in any other way. The same tools are used by people with very different levels of experience, often performing the same tasks within a similar timeframe. This makes it possible to observe how tools behave not only in one pair of hands, but across many.

Over time, certain patterns begin to appear.

I can see how a beginner struggles to stabilise a chisel, how someone else unconsciously compensates for a poorly balanced tool, or how fatigue builds up when a handle does not sit naturally in the hand. These are not isolated cases. They repeat.

What initially appears to be a question of comfort turns out, in practice, to directly influence control. A tool that forces constant adjustment, even in subtle ways, gradually takes attention away from the work itself. Instead of focusing on material and process, the user is occupied with maintaining stability.

## Balance and control

This becomes especially visible over longer periods of work.

A tool that feels acceptable at the beginning of a task may start to reveal its limitations after an hour or two. Small imbalances, barely noticeable at first, translate into tension in the hand, loss of precision, and inconsistent results.

A well-balanced tool does something quite different.

It does not draw attention to itself. It allows the user to stay focused on the process, rather than on the act of holding the tool.

## Conclusion

With time, it becomes clear that what we initially describe as comfort is in fact closely tied to control, because tools that sit naturally in the hand allow that control to be maintained consistently throughout the work.

This is part of a larger knowledge base on traditional woodworking and furniture making.
