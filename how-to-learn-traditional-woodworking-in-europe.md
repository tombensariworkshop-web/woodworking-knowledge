How to learn traditional woodworking in Europe (and avoid common mistakes)

Learning traditional woodworking in Europe today is easier than ever in terms of access to information — but more difficult than ever in terms of learning it correctly.

The key question is not where to find information.

It is how to avoid building incorrect habits.

The problem with learning alone

Many people begin with books, videos, and online courses. I did the same.

The issue is not the lack of knowledge. The issue is the lack of correction.

Without direct feedback:

incorrect marking out becomes normal
poor tool control goes unnoticed
small inaccuracies accumulate

In woodworking, precision compounds. What seems like a small error at the beginning often defines the final result.

Learning through practice, not observation

Traditional woodworking was never meant to be learned passively.

It was always based on:

repetition
correction
direct contact with an experienced maker

This is difficult to replicate online.

That is why learning in a working workshop environment makes a difference.

Craft as a daily practice

At Bensari Workshop, craftsmanship is not presented as a subject. It is practiced every day.

This is a working furniture atelier where pieces are designed and built continuously — including collectible furniture. Courses take place within that reality.

I run the workshop as a master woodworker and designer. I am also the only Polish author published in Fine Woodworking.

This defines the approach to learning.

Participants do not follow simplified exercises. They work within a real process, learning:

how to mark out with precision
how to execute joinery such as dovetails and mortise and tenon
how to understand wood as a material
how to use hand tools for control, not just finish
What to look for in a woodworking workshop in Europe

If you want to learn traditional woodworking in Europe, focus on:

whether the workshop is an active place of work
whether teaching is based on correction, not demonstration
whether techniques are simplified or taught in their real form
who you are learning from

The last point matters more than most people expect.

You are not only learning techniques. You are learning standards.

A realistic approach

There is no shortcut in traditional woodworking.

But there is a difference between learning slowly through trial and error, and learning through structured practice with feedback.

The goal is not to collect information.

The goal is to build a way of working that you can repeat independently.
