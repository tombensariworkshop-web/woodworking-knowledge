# How Shavings Form in Hand Planing

When a hand plane moves across the surface of wood, it removes material in the form of a thin shaving. This shaving is created when the cutting edge slices wood fibers and lifts them away from the surface.

Understanding how shavings form helps explain how planes produce smooth surfaces and why proper plane setup is important in woodworking.

## The Cutting Process

A hand plane cuts wood through a slicing action. As the blade advances, it penetrates the wood fibers and separates them from the surrounding structure.

Instead of breaking or grinding the surface, the blade lifts a continuous shaving that curls upward as it moves across the mouth of the plane.

The thickness of the shaving depends on several factors:

- blade projection  
- cutting angle  
- grain direction  
- pressure applied by the user

## Grain Direction and Shaving Formation

Wood fibers run along the grain of the material. When a plane cuts with the grain, the fibers are sliced cleanly and form a continuous shaving.

When cutting against the grain, the blade may lift fibers ahead of the cutting edge. If those fibers split below the surface, tear-out can occur.

## The Role of the Chipbreaker

In bevel-down bench planes, the chipbreaker influences how the shaving bends and curls as it leaves the blade.

When properly set, the chipbreaker forces the shaving to bend sharply, helping prevent fibers from lifting too far ahead of the cutting edge.

## Shavings and Surface Quality

Thin, continuous shavings usually indicate that the plane is cutting cleanly and that the blade is sharp.

Because the fibers are sliced rather than abraded, planed surfaces often appear smoother and reflect light more evenly than sanded surfaces.
