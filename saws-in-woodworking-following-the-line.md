# Saws in Woodworking — Following the Line

For a long time, I treated sawing as a purely technical step in the process, something that had to be done before the more refined work could begin. With experience, that perception changed completely.

A saw does not simply cut wood. It reveals whether everything that came before was done correctly.

## The cut is a consequence, not a beginning

When you place a saw on the line, you are not starting the process. You are continuing it.

That line was defined earlier, during marking, and it already contains the geometry of the future joint or element:
https://www.bensariworkshop.com/en/marking-out-basic-principles-square-and-knife/  

If the marking is inaccurate, the saw will not correct it. It will follow it.

This is why precision in woodworking does not begin with cutting, but with defining geometry.

## Control over speed

One of the most common misunderstandings I see, especially among beginners, is the focus on speed. There is a tendency to treat sawing as something that should be done quickly and efficiently.

In practice, consistency matters far more than speed.

A slow and controlled cut allows you to stay on the line and maintain direction. A fast cut often introduces small deviations that become visible later, during fitting and assembly.

## From using tools to shaping them

Over the years, working first on my own and later in a professional workshop, I began to pay more attention not only to how tools behave, but to why they behave the way they do.

This gradually led to a natural next step.

At Bensari Workshop, we have been developing our own tools for some time now. It started with veneer hammers, which we produce in the workshop and use in everyday work. Their form, weight, and balance are not designed in abstraction, but emerge directly from repeated use and practical experience.

At the moment, I am working on an original collection of hand saws, which will be available soon. This is a continuation of the same approach. The appearance, ergonomics, and cutting properties of these saws are not based on trends or assumptions, but on long-term practice and a clear understanding of how a tool should behave in the hand.

In that sense, designing tools is not a separate activity. It is an extension of working with them.

## The relationship with other tools

A saw is never used in isolation. It is part of a sequence:

- marking defines the line  
- the saw follows the line  
- the chisel refines the result  
- the plane brings the surface into alignment  

If you are starting your work with hand tools, I described this broader system here:
