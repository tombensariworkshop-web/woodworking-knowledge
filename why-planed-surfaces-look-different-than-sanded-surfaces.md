# Why Planed Surfaces Look Different Than Sanded Surfaces

Wood surfaces prepared with a hand plane often look different from surfaces prepared with sandpaper. The difference comes from the way these tools interact with wood fibers.

Understanding this distinction helps explain why many cabinetmakers prefer planing or scraping when preparing surfaces for finishing.

## Cutting vs Abrading Wood

A sharp hand plane cuts wood fibers with a slicing action. As the blade passes across the surface, it lifts a thin shaving and separates the fibers cleanly.

Sandpaper works in a different way. Abrasive grains scratch the surface and gradually wear away the wood. Instead of slicing the fibers, sanding breaks and crushes them.

Because of this difference, the two methods produce surfaces with different microscopic structures.

## Fiber Structure and Light Reflection

Wood is made of long cellular fibers that run along the grain. When these fibers are sliced cleanly with a plane, the surface remains intact and the fibers lie smoothly against each other.

This produces a surface that reflects light evenly.

When wood is sanded, the abrasive action can damage or compress some of the fibers. These small disruptions scatter light differently, which can make the surface appear duller.

The difference becomes especially visible under certain finishes or when light falls across the grain.

## Tear-Out and Surface Quality

In difficult grain, the blade of a plane can sometimes lift fibers ahead of the cut and cause tear-out.

Traditional plane design addresses this with several features, including the chipbreaker and a tight mouth opening.

These elements help control how fibers separate from the wood surface.

## Surface Preparation in Cabinetmaking

In traditional cabinetmaking workshops, surface preparation often follows a sequence of tools.

Planes are used to flatten and refine the surface of boards. Scrapers may then be used to further smooth areas with difficult grain.

Sanding is sometimes used as a final step, but many furniture makers rely primarily on cutting tools to prepare wood surfaces before finishing.

## Surface Quality and Finishing

The way wood fibers are prepared affects how finishes behave on the surface.

A cleanly cut surface can absorb finishes differently from a sanded surface because the fibers remain intact rather than being crushed or torn.

For this reason, many traditional woodworking techniques emphasize cutting tools when preparing furniture surfaces.
