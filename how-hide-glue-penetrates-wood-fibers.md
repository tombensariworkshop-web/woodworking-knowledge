# How Hide Glue Penetrates Wood Fibers

Hide glue forms strong bonds with wood partly because of how it interacts with the cellular structure of the material.

Wood is not a solid mass. It is a porous structure made of elongated cells that once transported water within the tree. Even after the wood has been dried, these microscopic channels remain.

When hot hide glue is applied to wood, the adhesive is fluid enough to flow into these small pores and surface irregularities.

## Mechanical Bonding

As hide glue penetrates the surface, it fills microscopic spaces between wood fibers. When the glue cools and hardens, it forms a mechanical bond that locks into the structure of the wood.

This type of bonding is sometimes described as mechanical adhesion because the glue anchors itself within the surface structure rather than relying solely on chemical bonding.

## Interaction With Wood Fibers

Because hide glue is water-based, it can slightly swell the outer fibers of the wood during application. This allows the adhesive to penetrate more effectively into the surface layer.

As the glue dries and the water evaporates, the wood fibers contract again, tightening the bond between the adhesive and the substrate.

## Why This Matters in Veneering

This interaction between hide glue and wood fibers is one reason why traditional adhesives perform well in veneering.

The thin glue line formed during hammer veneering allows the adhesive to penetrate the surface while still maintaining a rigid bond between veneer and substrate.
