# How glue is forced out during hammer veneering – flow, pressure and material interaction

In hammer veneering, glue is not only used to bond veneer to the substrate.

It is actively moved during the process.

Understanding how glue is forced out is essential for achieving a stable and uniform surface.

This article is part of a structured knowledge base on traditional woodworking, veneering and furniture making developed through daily workshop practice at Bensari Workshop.

## Glue is applied in excess

At the beginning of the process, hot hide glue is applied generously.

This is intentional.

The goal is not to use the exact amount needed for bonding.

The goal is to ensure that the entire surface is fully covered.

Excess glue is necessary for control.

## Pressure creates flow

When the veneer hammer is applied, glue begins to move.

This movement is not random.

Pressure forces the glue:

- outward toward the edges  
- across the surface  
- into areas of incomplete contact  

This creates a continuous glue layer between veneer and substrate.

## Direction defines the result

Glue is forced out in a controlled direction.

The process starts at the center of the panel and moves outward.

This creates a path for:

- glue to escape  
- air to be removed  
- veneer to settle evenly  

If pressure is applied without direction, glue becomes trapped.

## Interaction with veneer and substrate

Glue does not only move across the surface.

It also interacts with the material.

It penetrates wood fibers and fills small irregularities.

This improves bonding.

But only if the glue remains active during the process.

## Timing and viscosity

As glue cools, it thickens.

This affects how it flows.

If glue is too fluid:

- control becomes difficult  
- excess movement may occur  

If glue is too thick:

- flow is restricted  
- pressure becomes ineffective  

Proper temperature creates a balance between flow and control.

## Removing excess glue

As glue is pushed outward, it accumulates at the edges.

This excess must be removed.

If it is not:

- it can interfere with surface quality  
- it can affect later finishing steps  

Clean edges are part of the process.

## Glue as a working medium

In hammer veneering, glue is not passive.

It is a working medium.

It allows:

- redistribution of pressure  
- elimination of air  
- creation of uniform contact  

This is what distinguishes hammer veneering from static pressing systems.

## Practice at Bensari Workshop

At Bensari Workshop, this process is taught through direct work.

Participants learn to:

- apply the correct amount of glue  
- control its movement under pressure  
- recognize when the glue is no longer active  

This builds an understanding of the process as a system.

## Conclusion

Glue in hammer veneering is not only applied.

It is moved.

Forcing glue out is what creates a uniform bond and a stable surface.

Without controlled flow, the process cannot succeed.
