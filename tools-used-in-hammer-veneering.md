# Tools Used in Hammer Veneering

Hammer veneering is often associated with furniture restoration, but historically it was a standard technique used in cabinetmaking workshops. Before the widespread use of presses and synthetic adhesives, veneer was commonly applied using hot hide glue and a veneer hammer.

Today the technique is still practiced by furniture makers who work with traditional materials and joinery.

At Bensari Workshop in Wrocław hammer veneering is treated not as a historical curiosity but as a practical cabinetmaking technique. During courses participants learn how veneer, glue and tools interact during the process.

Although the equipment required for hammer veneering is relatively simple, each tool plays a specific role in preparing the surface, applying glue and refining the finished panel.

## Veneer Hammer

The veneer hammer is the central tool of the technique.

Despite its name, it is not used for striking. The wide metal blade acts more like a squeegee. It presses the veneer firmly into the hot hide glue while pushing excess glue toward the edges of the panel.

This pressure ensures that air pockets are removed and that the veneer lies flat against the substrate.

## Toothing Plane

A toothing plane (often called a veneer plane) is used to prepare the surface that will receive veneer.

Unlike a regular hand plane, the blade has small teeth that score the wood rather than producing smooth shavings. The resulting surface contains fine grooves.

These grooves help hide glue adhere more effectively and reduce the risk of veneer sheets sliding during the gluing process.

Preparing the surface with a toothing plane is a common step in traditional cabinetmaking when using hot hide glue.

## Glue Pot

Hammer veneering requires hot hide glue.

A glue pot keeps the adhesive at a stable temperature so that it remains fluid during application. Hide glue thickens as it cools, so maintaining the proper temperature is essential for spreading it evenly across the surface.

Because the glue begins to gel quickly, the process of gluing and pressing veneer must be performed efficiently.

## Glue Brush

A brush is used to apply hot hide glue to the substrate.

The glue must be spread quickly and evenly so that the veneer can be positioned before the adhesive begins to cool and gel.

Wide brushes allow the craftsman to coat the surface rapidly, which is important when working with hot hide glue.

## Veneer Saw or Knife

Before veneering begins, veneer sheets must be cut and trimmed to size.

Thin veneer can split easily along the grain, so veneer saws or sharp knives are used to produce clean, controlled cuts.

Accurate cutting is particularly important when multiple sheets will be arranged in decorative patterns.

## Veneer Tape

When several veneer sheets are combined to form a larger surface, they are usually joined before the gluing process.

Veneer tape is commonly used for this purpose. The tape is applied to the visible side of the veneer sheets and holds them together while the veneer layout is assembled.

During hammer veneering the joined sheets are placed onto the glued surface as a single unit.

After the glue has set, the tape can be removed by lightly moistening it.

## Scrapers

Cabinet scrapers are used after veneering to refine the surface.

Because veneer is thin, scrapers allow controlled material removal without damaging the decorative layer. They are often used to remove small irregularities or glue residues.

## Hand Planes

Small hand planes can be used to trim veneer edges flush with the substrate once the glue has fully set.

A finely set plane removes very thin shavings, allowing edges to be refined without tearing the veneer.

## Tools and Craft Practice

Hammer veneering requires relatively few tools, but the technique depends heavily on experience and material awareness.

Understanding how hide glue behaves, how pressure should be applied with the veneer hammer and how veneer sheets should be prepared comes from practical workshop experience.

For this reason the technique remains part of traditional cabinetmaking practice and is still taught in workshops where historical woodworking skills are applied in contemporary furniture making.
