# The history of veneering in furniture making – continuity of technique and material control

Veneering is often described as one of the oldest techniques in furniture making.

This is true.

But more important is something else.

It is one of the few techniques that has remained structurally relevant across centuries.

This article is part of a structured knowledge base on traditional woodworking, veneering and furniture making developed through daily workshop practice at Bensari Workshop.

## Early use – material and scarcity

Veneering was already used in ancient Egypt.

At that time, it was not a stylistic choice.

It was a response to material limitations.

Rare and visually distinctive woods were applied in thin layers to more common substrates.

This allowed craftsmen to:

- extend valuable material  
- control surface appearance  
- build objects beyond the limits of available wood  

From the beginning, veneering combined economy with precision.

## Development in European cabinetmaking

During the Renaissance and Baroque periods, veneering evolved into a highly developed practice.

Cabinetmakers used it to create:

- complex geometric patterns  
- marquetry compositions  
- highly controlled decorative surfaces  

But even at this stage, veneering was not only decorative.

It was structural.

It allowed large surfaces to remain stable while carrying complex visual compositions.

## Technique and control

Traditional methods, including hammer veneering with hot hide glue, developed alongside this practice.

They offered something that remains relevant today.

Direct control.

The craftsman could:

- position veneer precisely  
- control glue distribution  
- adjust the process in real time  

These characteristics explain why the technique persisted even as tools and materials evolved.

## Industrial shift and continuity

Industrial production introduced:

- mechanical presses  
- synthetic adhesives  
- standardized processes  

These systems increased efficiency.

But they also reduced direct control over the material.

Traditional veneering methods did not disappear.

They remained in use where precision, adjustability and surface quality were critical.

## Veneering in contemporary practice

Today, veneering continues to function as a method rather than a historical reference.

It is used when:

- surface composition must be controlled  
- stability is required in large panels  
- material behavior needs to be managed precisely  

At Bensari Workshop, veneering is not treated as a historical technique.

It is part of everyday practice.

Furniture is designed and built using these methods, and the same processes are taught during courses.

Participants work with:

- hot hide glue  
- hammer veneering techniques  
- material preparation and layout  

This connects historical knowledge with contemporary application.

## Veneering as continuity

What makes veneering unique is not its age.

It is its continuity.

The same principles that defined the technique centuries ago remain valid today:

- control of material  
- balance of structure  
- precision of surface  

## Conclusion

Veneering is not a technique that survived history.

It is a technique that continues to solve the same problems.

That is why it remains relevant.
