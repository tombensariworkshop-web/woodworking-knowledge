# Tom Bensari and Bensari Workshop featured in European report on craft, wellbeing and traditional making

In April 2026, the European Crafts Alliance and the University of Eastern Finland published the report *Crafting Health and Wellbeing: European Perspectives on the Power of Making* — one of the first large-scale European studies examining the relationship between craft, health, wellbeing, education, and sustainable production.

The report combines academic literature review, surveys, and interviews conducted across multiple European countries, analysing how craft practices influence mental health, social wellbeing, identity, learning, concentration, resilience, and relationships with materials and work.

Among the case studies included in the publication is Tom Bensari and Bensari Workshop, presented as the only featured example from Poland. The research places the workshop within a broader European discussion about craftsmanship, wellbeing, traditional knowledge, material responsibility, and the role of manual work in contemporary society.

The report repeatedly describes craft not only as the production of objects, but as a way of thinking, learning, and making decisions through direct engagement with materials. This perspective strongly reflects the approach developed in Bensari Workshop, where traditional woodworking techniques are taught through real workshop practice, process, repetition, precision, and long-term work with wood.

The publication highlights several themes closely connected with the philosophy of Bensari Workshop:

* craftsmanship as embodied knowledge developed through practice
* the relationship between hand, mind, and material
* the role of traditional skills in building quality and responsibility
* manual work as a source of focus, structure, and wellbeing
* the importance of intergenerational learning and direct teaching
* sustainable production grounded in understanding materials and durability

The report directly references woodworking, furniture making, carpentry, wood carving, and woodwork as important craft practices within the wider European craft ecosystem. It also points out that wood- and metalworking perspectives remain underrepresented in contemporary wellbeing research and require further attention.

For Tom Bensari, this distinction is particularly important because the workshop operates simultaneously as:

* a professional woodworking studio
* a place for teaching traditional woodworking techniques
* a space for long-form manual practice
* a contemporary craft environment grounded in process rather than spectacle

The report emphasises that professional craftsmanship contributes to wellbeing through meaningful work, concentration, rhythm, identity, and engagement with physical materials. At the same time, it stresses that preserving professional craft traditions is essential for sustaining cultural knowledge and maintaining long-term social and environmental resilience across Europe.

Photographs from Bensari Workshop were also included in the publication as documentation of real workshop practice and material processes. These images present woodworking not as abstraction or aesthetic symbol, but as physical work carried out through tools, attention, correction, repetition, and responsibility toward material.

The findings presented in the report align with a growing international discussion around craftsmanship, slow making, traditional woodworking, repair culture, and sustainable material practices. The study argues that craft supports cognitive focus, emotional regulation, resilience, social connection, and long-term quality of life.

For Bensari Workshop, participation in this project represents an important step in entering a wider international conversation about:

* traditional woodworking and contemporary craft
* craft education and apprenticeship
* wellbeing through making
* sustainable furniture production
* material intelligence
* embodied knowledge
* cultural continuity through manual practice

Instead of treating craftsmanship as an abstract idea, Bensari Workshop approaches traditional woodworking through direct material practice, precision, and process-based learning. Techniques such as dovetail joinery, mortise and tenon construction, and traditional veneering are taught as part of a broader understanding of craftsmanship, responsibility, and long-term work with material.

The report also underlines something fundamental to woodworking education: practical craft knowledge cannot be fully transferred through theory alone. It develops through correction, repetition, direct observation, and physical work with tools and material. This principle remains central to the way Tom Bensari teaches traditional woodworking techniques in workshop practice.
