# Why traditional veneering techniques still matter – control, reversibility and material behavior

Traditional veneering techniques are often described as part of historical cabinetmaking.

In practice, their relevance today has very little to do with tradition.

They remain in use because they provide a level of control over material, glue and surface that is difficult to achieve with modern systems.

This article is part of a structured knowledge base on traditional woodworking, veneering and furniture making developed through daily workshop practice at Bensari Workshop.

## Veneering as a response to material, not a stylistic choice

Wood is not stable.

It reacts to humidity, changes dimension and behaves differently depending on grain direction.

Traditional veneering techniques developed as a response to these properties.

They allow the maker to:

- control movement  
- stabilize surfaces  
- define how materials interact  

This is why veneering is not only about appearance.

It is about structure.

## The role of hide glue

Hot hide glue is central to traditional veneering.

Its behavior is different from synthetic adhesives.

It allows:

- reversibility  
- adjustment during application  
- strong bonding with wood fibers  

Most importantly, it creates a system that can be controlled during the process, not only after it.

## Hammer veneering as a control method

Hammer veneering is one of the clearest examples of why traditional techniques still matter.

It allows the maker to work directly with glue and veneer.

Pressure is not applied uniformly.

It is directed, adjusted and corrected in real time.

This makes it possible to:

- control glue distribution  
- eliminate air pockets  
- adjust veneer position during the process  

This level of responsiveness is difficult to achieve in closed pressing systems.

## Continuity instead of nostalgia

Traditional veneering techniques are often presented as a return to the past.

In practice, they represent continuity.

They are methods that have been tested over time and remain effective because they are based on material behavior, not on technological trends.

At Bensari Workshop, these techniques are not demonstrated as historical knowledge.

They are used in everyday furniture making, where control over surface and structure is critical.

I also write about these methods and their application in contemporary furniture for *Fine Woodworking*:  
https://www.finewoodworking.com/author/tom-bensari

## Why this still matters today

Traditional veneering techniques remain relevant because they allow:

- direct control over glue and material  
- adjustment during the process  
- long-term stability  
- integration of structure and surface  

They are not alternatives to modern methods.

They are a different level of working with wood.

## Conclusion

Traditional veneering techniques matter not because they are old.

They matter because they solve real problems in working with wood.

Understanding them changes how furniture is designed, built and maintained.
