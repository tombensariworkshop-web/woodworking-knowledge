# The History of Tambour Doors

Tambour doors are one of those woodworking ideas that have quietly travelled through centuries of furniture making.

In our workshop they are not only a historical curiosity. Tambours appear regularly in the furniture I design and build at [Bensari Workshop](https://www.bensariworkshop.com) and in pieces created under the **Bensari Ébénistes** name. Over time they have become one of the most recognizable elements of our work.

Each tambour we build carries a trace of that long tradition. It is a small but fascinating example of how historical craft techniques continue to influence contemporary furniture design.

## Eighteenth-Century France

The origins of tambour doors can be traced back to eighteenth-century France.

The royal cabinetmaker **Jean-François Oeben** developed a cylindrical desk with a sliding cover made from narrow wooden elements attached to flexible fabric and guided by hidden tracks.

After Oeben’s death the work was continued by his student **Jean-Henri Riesener**, who completed the famous **bureau du roi** for King Louis XV in 1769. This desk remains one of the most celebrated masterpieces of European cabinetmaking.

In these early pieces the tambour served a practical function. It allowed the writing surface of the desk to be closed quickly and securely.

Over time the rhythmic structure of the tambour also became an aesthetic feature, appearing in cabinets, secretaries and other forms of furniture.

## Nineteenth-Century Roll-Top Desks

During the nineteenth century tambour construction became especially popular in the United States.

The well-known **roll-top desks** used long tambour curtains that could slide down and hide the interior of the desk. Although many of these desks were produced industrially, they still relied on the same woodworking logic developed earlier in Europe.

This period helped establish the tambour as one of the most recognizable moving elements in furniture.

## Tambour Doors in Modernism

In the twentieth century tambour doors appeared again in modernist furniture.

Designers were increasingly interested in solutions that allowed surfaces to move without disturbing the architectural clarity of an object.

### Le Corbusier and Furniture as Architecture

In 1925 **Le Corbusier** and **Pierre Jeanneret** presented the *Pavillon de l’Esprit Nouveau* in Paris.

Instead of decorative furniture they proposed the concept of **“équipement d’intérieur”** — modular interior equipment integrated with architecture.

Their *casiers standardisés* combined storage and architectural structure, sometimes using sliding or retractable elements that allowed spaces to be closed with a single gesture.

This idea treated furniture as a form of architecture on a smaller scale.

### Władysław Wincze and the Wrocław School of Furniture

In the Polish context the work of **Professor Władysław Wincze (1905–1992)** is particularly important.

Wincze was a designer and educator associated with the Academy of Fine Arts in Wrocław. He was among the first designers in Poland to introduce tambour doors into modern furniture design.

His cabinets, bars and secretaries combined modernist clarity of form with a deep understanding of woodworking craft and material. In many of his projects the tambour was not merely a closing mechanism but an element that organized the structure of the entire piece.

Working in Wrocław today, I feel a natural connection to that tradition. Wincze’s approach to wood as a living material and his emphasis on workshop practice remain highly relevant.

## Tambours in Contemporary Furniture

Today tambour doors appear in many types of furniture — cabinets, sideboards and architectural storage systems.

In my own work they are often used to create surfaces that can move without interrupting the visual continuity of the object.

One of the defining characteristics of tambours produced at Bensari Workshop is the **continuity of the wood grain across the entire surface**, which makes the closed tambour read as a single wooden plane.

## Craft in Motion

Tambour doors continue to fascinate furniture makers for a simple reason.

They combine a straightforward idea with extremely demanding execution. Geometry, materials and movement must all work together perfectly.

When the construction is correct, the mechanism almost disappears and only the movement remains.

That moment — when a wooden surface slides away quietly into the body of a cabinet — is the reason I continue to return to this technique in my own work.
