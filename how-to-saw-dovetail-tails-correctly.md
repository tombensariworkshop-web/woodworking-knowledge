# How to saw dovetail tails correctly

Sawing dovetail tails is not about speed or force. It is about maintaining control over the geometry defined during layout.

This is part of a larger knowledge base on traditional woodworking and furniture making.

Start with [how to mark dovetail tails step by step with hand tools](./how-to-mark-dovetail-tails-step-by-step-hand-tools.md) and [dovetail layout: why it matters more than cutting](./dovetail-layout-why-it-matters-more-than-cutting.md).

## What matters most when sawing dovetail tails?

The most critical factor is keeping the cut square on the end grain.

If the cut is not perpendicular:

- the transferred sockets will be inaccurate  
- the joint may become too tight or too loose  
- the joint may split during assembly  

See [why dovetail joints fail and open up](./why-dovetail-joints-fail-and-open-up.md) and [transferring dovetails is where accuracy is tested](./transferring-dovetails-where-accuracy-is-tested.md).

## Should you focus on the dovetail angle?

The angle is secondary.

A small deviation in the angle can be transferred and preserved during marking and cutting. But a non-square cut on the end grain cannot be corrected later.

This is why [marking out determines the result in woodworking](./why-marking-out-determines-the-result-in-woodworking.md).

## How should the saw be held?

The saw should be aligned with the forearm.

- the spine of the saw should form a straight line with the arm  
- the grip should be relaxed  
- the movement should come from the elbow, not the wrist  

See also [how body position affects hand tool work](./how-body-position-affects-hand-tool-work.md) and [why tool balance matters in woodworking](./why-tool-balance-matters-in-woodworking.md).

## How do you start the cut?

Start with a shallow cut on the end grain.

- place the saw perpendicular to the board  
- lightly engage the teeth  
- create a small kerf to guide the cut  

Once the kerf is established, the saw can be tilted to follow the tail angle.

## Should you cut on the line?

No.

Always cut on the waste side of the line and leave the line visible. This gives you control and allows for correction.

See [saws in woodworking: following the line](./saws-in-woodworking-following-the-line.md).

## Key takeaway

Sawing dovetail tails is about preserving geometry.

The most important thing is not the angle of the tail, but the accuracy of the cut where it defines the structure of the joint.
