# How to saw dovetail tails correctly

Sawing dovetail tails is often described as a matter of hand control. In practice, it is more precise to say that it is a matter of alignment, reference, and consistency.

The saw does not create accuracy. It reveals whether the work prepared before it was clear and stable.

At Bensari Workshop, this stage is approached as part of a continuous process. The way the tails are marked determines how they can be cut. The way they are cut determines whether they can be transferred correctly.

## What matters before the saw touches the wood

Before cutting begins, several conditions need to be established:

- a clear baseline
- well-defined tail geometry
- stable reference faces and edges

If these are not in place, the cut becomes an attempt to correct earlier uncertainty.

A clear explanation of how I establish this layout in practice can be found here:
https://www.bensariworkshop.com/en/dovetail-layout-hand-cut-guide/

## Controlling the cut

When sawing dovetail tails, the goal is not speed or force. It is consistency.

The saw should follow the line in a controlled manner, while maintaining a square cut on the end grain. This is where many errors occur. A cut can appear correct from one face while already drifting out of square.

The body position, grip, and starting angle all contribute to whether the cut remains controlled.

## What beginners often misunderstand

Many people focus on staying exactly on the line from the visible face. In practice, what matters more is the relationship between both faces and the end grain.

A cut that slightly touches the line but remains square is more reliable than a cut that follows the line visually but is not aligned.

## Sawing as part of a system

Sawing is not an isolated skill. It is connected to:

- marking out
- transferring the joint
- final fitting

Understanding these relationships changes how the saw is used.
