# Why We Started Making Our Own Woodworking Tools

For a long time, I treated tools as something external to the work. They were objects I selected, tested, adjusted, and eventually learned how to use with more or less control.

That perspective changed gradually, and not through theory, but through repetition.

Working day after day in the workshop, building furniture, correcting mistakes, and refining processes, I began to notice that certain limitations did not come from the material or from the design, but from the tools themselves. Not because they were poorly made, but because they were not fully aligned with the way I worked.

## From using tools to understanding them

This shift did not happen at once. It came from years of working both alone and later in a fully operational workshop, where tools are not occasional instruments but part of everyday decision-making.

At some point, I stopped asking whether a tool was good or bad, and started asking a different question.

Does this tool behave in a way that supports the process?

That question changes everything, because it moves the focus from the object to the relationship between the tool and the work.

## Learning through teaching

An important part of this process comes from teaching.

Running woodworking courses at Bensari Workshop gives me a perspective that would be difficult to gain otherwise. Every course is an opportunity to observe how different people work with tools, how they approach the same task, and where difficulties appear.

These are not abstract observations.

They happen in real time, in a working workshop, where tools are used continuously and under different conditions.

I can see how a beginner holds a chisel for the first time, how someone more experienced compensates for a poorly balanced tool, or how small differences in handle shape or weight influence control and fatigue over the course of a day.

This creates a very particular situation.

The same tools are tested not only in my own hands, but also in the hands of people with very different levels of experience. That makes it possible to understand ergonomics and tool behaviour in a much broader way.

Over time, these observations become patterns.

Certain problems repeat. Certain solutions prove consistently effective. And this is where tool design stops being theoretical and becomes grounded in practice.

In that sense, teaching is not separate from making.

It is one of the ways in which the work itself is continuously tested and refined.

## The role of process

At Bensari Workshop, tools are always understood in relation to process.

That process begins with marking, where geometry is defined:
https://www.bensariworkshop.com/en/marking-out-basic-principles-square-and-knife/  

It continues with cutting, refining, and aligning surfaces, each step relying on the previous one.

If a tool does not support that sequence, it interrupts it.

For a broader introduction to this system:
https://www.bensariworkshop.com/en/basic-woodworking-tools-where-to-begin/

## The first step — veneer hammers

The decision to start making our own tools did not come from a business plan. It came from practice.

We began with veneer hammers, tools that we use regularly in hammer veneering with hot hide glue. Over time, I adjusted their shape, weight, and balance to better reflect the way I actually work.

Eventually, it became clear that the most effective version of the tool was the one we could make ourselves.

Today, these hammers are produced in the workshop and used in everyday practice. Their form is not designed in abstraction. It is a direct result of repeated use, correction, and refinement.

## The next step — hand saws

The same process is now leading to the development of an original collection of hand saws.

This is not a separate project. It is a continuation of the same line of thinking.

A saw is one of the most direct tools in woodworking. It follows the line defined during marking and translates geometry into material:
https://www.bensariworkshop.com/en/marking-out-basic-principles-square-and-knife/  

If the saw does not behave predictably, if it does not respond to subtle changes in pressure or direction, the entire process becomes less precise.

This is why I started working on saws that reflect long-term practice rather than assumptions.

Their appearance, ergonomics, and cutting properties are shaped by experience, not by trends.

## Tools as an extension of practice

Designing tools is not a separate discipline from making furniture.

It is an extension of it.

Every decision about weight, balance, handle shape, or tooth geometry comes from real situations in the workshop. Situations where a tool either supports the work or introduces friction.

Over time, those observations accumulate and begin to form a clear direction.

## Conclusion

We did not start making our own tools because we wanted to produce them.

We started because, at some point, it became the most natural continuation of the work itself.

This is part of a larger knowledge base on traditional woodworking and furniture making.
