# Common mistakes in dovetail marking out

When dovetails do not fit, the instinct is to blame the cut.

In practice, the problem almost always starts earlier, at the stage of marking out, even if at first everything seems correct and under control.

This article is part of a structured knowledge base on traditional woodworking, joinery, and furniture making developed through daily workshop practice at Bensari Workshop.

Over the years, both in my own work and while teaching, I have seen the same situations repeat. Someone carefully cuts along the line, works with attention, uses sharp tools, and still, when the joint is assembled, something starts to drift. The elements do not come together cleanly, the baseline does not align, or the joint closes under tension.

At that point, there is nothing left to correct.

The decision has already been made earlier.

## Where things actually go wrong

Most mistakes do not come from a lack of skill with tools, but from a lack of clarity at the marking out stage.

It often begins with something small. The baseline is transferred slightly inconsistently. The reference edge is not treated as a constant point of control. The lines are drawn correctly in isolation, but they do not fully relate to each other.

In the moment, these differences seem insignificant.

In assembly, they accumulate.

This is why marking out is not about drawing lines, but about defining relationships between elements:  
https://www.bensariworkshop.com/en/dovetail-layout-hand-cut-guide/

## Losing reference

One of the most common problems I see is losing reference edges during the process.

At the beginning everything is clear. The elements are marked, the triangle is there, the faces are defined. But as work progresses, attention shifts to individual operations.

Somewhere along the way, the reference system quietly disappears.

From that point on, each action introduces a small deviation. Measurements are still correct, tools are used properly, but the work no longer relates to a single consistent reference.

The result is a joint that looks right in parts, but does not function as a whole.

Working from one face and one edge is not a rule for beginners. It is a way of maintaining control throughout the entire process:

[Reference edges in woodworking](reference-edges-in-woodworking.md)

## The illusion of precision

Another mistake comes from confusing sharp tools with accuracy.

A well-prepared saw, a sharp chisel, a clean cut — all of this gives confidence. But it does not guarantee a good result.

If the geometry is not clearly defined during marking out, the tools will only follow that uncertainty.

Precision in dovetails does not come from how thin the line is.

It comes from how clearly the relationship between elements is defined.

## What actually changes the result

Avoiding these mistakes is not about adding more tools or following more rules.

It is about simplifying the process and making it consistent.

In practice, this means working from clearly defined reference edges, ensuring that all lines relate to the same system, and treating marking out as a stage where the joint is already defined, not prepared.

When this is clear, cutting becomes predictable.

When it is not, correction becomes inevitable.
