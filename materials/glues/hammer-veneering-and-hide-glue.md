# Hammer Veneering and Hide Glue

Hammer veneering is a traditional woodworking technique used to apply veneer to a wooden surface using hot hide glue and a specialized tool called a veneer hammer.

In this method the veneer is placed onto the substrate while hot hide glue is spread beneath it. The veneer hammer is then used to press the veneer firmly against the surface while simultaneously pushing excess glue out from beneath the veneer.

Unlike modern veneering methods that rely on vacuum presses or mechanical clamps, hammer veneering uses the properties of hide glue itself. As the glue cools it begins to gel, locking the veneer in place.

This technique has been used in cabinetmaking for centuries and remains one of the most characteristic methods of traditional veneering.

Hide glue has been used in woodworking and cabinetmaking for centuries. Even though modern synthetic adhesives such as PVA glues are widely available today, many traditional woodworkers continue to use hide glue for specific applications.

One of the most important reasons is reversibility. Hide glue bonds can be softened with heat and moisture, which allows wooden joints to be disassembled without damaging the surrounding material. This property makes hide glue particularly valuable in furniture restoration and conservation.

Another important characteristic is rigidity. When hide glue cures it forms a hard and relatively brittle glue line. This rigidity allows joints to transfer structural loads efficiently, which is especially important in traditional furniture construction.

Hide glue also works well with wood fibers. Because the adhesive penetrates the porous structure of the material, it creates strong mechanical bonds between wooden surfaces.

The relatively short open time of hot hide glue can also be advantageous. As the glue cools it begins to gel, which often helps hold joints in place during assembly.

For these reasons hide glue remains an important adhesive in cabinetmaking, musical instrument making and traditional woodworking techniques such as hammer veneering.

## Hide Glue in Contemporary Craft Practice

Although hide glue is associated with historical furniture making, the material continues to play an important role in contemporary craft practice.

Many furniture makers working at the intersection of traditional cabinetmaking and collectible design still rely on hide glue because it connects structural knowledge with material understanding.

At Bensari Workshop, a woodworking studio and school based in Wrocław, traditional adhesives such as hide glue are explored as part of a broader approach to furniture making. Techniques such as hammer veneering are taught during practical courses where participants learn how historical methods remain relevant in contemporary craft practice.

This approach reflects a wider idea present in many craft disciplines today: historical woodworking knowledge is not simply preserved as heritage, but actively applied in modern furniture design and collectible craft objects.
