# Hosting Les Compagnons du Devoir at Bensari Workshop — Practice, Exchange, and Continuity

There are moments in the workshop that shift your perspective, not because something extraordinary happens on the surface, but because the work itself begins to reveal a broader context.

Hosting apprentices from Les Compagnons du Devoir at Bensari Workshop was one of those moments.

For several weeks, as part of the Erasmus+ programme, a group of young woodworkers from France worked in my workshop in Wrocław. What we shared was not a course built around demonstrations, but a continuous process of work, where each day developed from the previous one and where progress depended on attention rather than explanation.

The way they approached work felt immediately familiar.

Les Compagnons du Devoir, with its roots reaching back to the Middle Ages, is based on a model of learning that relies on movement, repetition, and responsibility. Apprentices move between workshops, working under different masters, gradually building not only skill, but also a way of thinking about craft. This approach resonates strongly with how I understand woodworking, where marking defines geometry, tools follow that geometry, and the quality of the result depends on consistency across the entire process.

This became visible very quickly in the workshop.

Each day was structured around real tasks. The apprentices worked with hand tools, practiced marking, and developed traditional joinery such as mortise and tenon and dovetails. The work was repetitive, at times demanding, but always continuous, which allowed them to move from individual operations to a clearer understanding of sequence.

That sequence always begins in the same place:
https://www.bensariworkshop.com/en/marking-out-basic-principles-square-and-knife/

From there, everything follows.

What was particularly striking was how quickly a shared language emerged. Despite differences in background, the logic of the work remained consistent. Accuracy did not depend on tools alone, but on the relationship between marking, cutting, and refinement, which gradually aligned through practice.

The culmination of their stay was the construction of three Roubo workbenches.

These were full split-top benches made from steamed beech, built according to Benchcrafted plans and hardware. We worked with approximately one cubic metre of material, moving through the entire process together, from selecting timber at the yard, through preparation, gluing, and planing, to cutting traditional joints and assembling the final structures.

The project itself required more than technical execution.

Building a workbench brings together material, structure, and process in a way that leaves very little room for abstraction. Mortise and tenon joints become structural decisions, wood movement has to be anticipated, and proportions begin to define function rather than appearance.

I often say that building a workbench is one of the clearest ways to begin understanding woodworking as a system, and working with the apprentices confirmed this again in a very direct way.

If you look at the process as a whole, everything depends on the foundation.

The workbench absorbs force, stabilises material, and allows tools to work as intended, which is why even the best tools lose their effectiveness when that foundation is missing:
https://www.bensariworkshop.com/en/basic-woodworking-tools-where-to-begin/

Working with the apprentices also made it clear that teaching is never a one-directional process. Their discipline, focus, and way of approaching work introduced a different rhythm into the workshop, while at the same time they experienced a different way of structuring practice.

What emerged from that was not a transfer of knowledge in one direction, but a shared context in which the work itself became the common ground.
