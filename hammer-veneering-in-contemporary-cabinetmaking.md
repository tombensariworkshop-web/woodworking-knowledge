# Hammer veneering in contemporary cabinetmaking – control, tradition, and real workshop practice

Hammer veneering is often described as a traditional technique associated with restoration or historical furniture.

In practice, it remains one of the most precise methods of controlling veneer, glue behavior and surface stability in contemporary cabinetmaking.

At Bensari Workshop, it is not treated as a historical reference.

It is part of everyday work.

This article is part of a structured knowledge base on traditional woodworking, joinery and furniture making developed through daily workshop practice at Bensari Workshop.

## Not a historical technique, but a working method

Hammer veneering allows direct control over the process.

You do not delegate pressure to a machine. You do not rely on a closed system.

You control:

- how the glue behaves  
- how pressure is applied  
- how the veneer is positioned  
- how the surface is formed  

This becomes especially important when working with figured veneers, complex grain or elements where surface quality defines the final result.

To understand this approach in a broader context:

## Two techniques, one system

At Bensari Workshop, hammer veneering is taught together with steam bending.

These are two techniques that have been part of cabinetmaking and ébéniste practice for centuries.

We do not treat them as separate skills.

They form one system of working with material.

Both require:

- understanding how wood behaves  
- controlling material under changing conditions  
- working with time, temperature and sequence  

This is not demonstration.

This is real work.

## Learning the full process

During the workshop, participants work through the complete sequence:

- preparing wood and veneer  
- applying veneer with hot hide glue  
- controlling pressure and removing excess glue  
- bending wood using steam  
- forming elements and stabilizing geometry  
- finishing surfaces to reveal the structure of the material  

This is not an introduction.

It is a process.

## Steam bending – where timing decides everything

The steam bending part of the course is particularly demanding.

Participants learn:

- how to build a steam chamber  
- how to prepare bending forms  
- how compression straps work and why they prevent failure  
- how to plan the sequence of operations  
- why timing is critical at every stage  

In practice, success depends on details.

Temperature. Moisture. Timing. Speed of action.

These cannot be understood without doing.

## Tools and materials in real use

During the course, we work with the same tools and materials used in the workshop:

- natural hide glue  
- veneering hammers made in our workshop  
- toothed planes for surface preparation  
- veneer tapes of different strengths  
- shellac finishes that reveal the structure of the wood  

We also discuss what actually matters in a workshop.

What to invest in. What can be made. What is unnecessary.

## Why this still matters

Hammer veneering and steam bending remain relevant because they solve problems that modern methods often simplify.

They allow:

- precise control over surface  
- structural continuity in curved elements  
- direct interaction with material  

They are not alternatives to modern tools.

They are part of a complete system of working.
