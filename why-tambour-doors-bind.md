# Why Tambour Doors Bind — and How We Prevent It

One of the most common problems with tambour doors is binding.

A tambour may look simple, but in practice it behaves more like a small mechanical system made from wood. When everything is built correctly the door moves quietly and almost effortlessly. When something is slightly wrong, the tambour will immediately reveal it.

In our work at [Bensari Workshop](https://www.bensariworkshop.com), tambours appear regularly in the furniture I design and build. Over time I have learned that most problems with tambours come down to a few key factors.

## Geometry

The most frequent cause of binding is geometry.

The tracks, the radius of the curve and the spacing of the tambour elements must work together precisely. Even a very small deviation can create friction when the tambour enters the curved section of the track.

Tambours are unforgiving in this respect. Hinged doors can tolerate small inaccuracies. Tambours cannot.

When designing furniture with tambour doors, I pay particular attention to the geometry of the tracks and their relationship to the cabinet structure.

## Tracks and Guiding Systems

The way the tambour is guided inside the cabinet is also crucial.

In our workshop we use two different solutions depending on the size of the tambour.

For **smaller tambours**, the door moves in **grooves milled directly into the wooden structure of the cabinet**. This solution works well when the tambour is relatively short and light.

For **larger tambours**, we use **brass tracks**. The tambour runs along precisely installed brass guides that ensure smooth and reliable movement over longer distances.

Brass performs very well in this role because it is durable, stable and provides a consistent surface for movement.

Choosing the right guiding system is one of the most important decisions when designing a tambour cabinet.

## Material Stability

Another common problem comes from material movement.

Tambours made from solid wood elements can react strongly to humidity changes. Even slight movement may affect the spacing between elements and disturb the movement of the door.

For this reason our tambours are built on a **plywood base which is then veneered**. Plywood provides a stable multilayer structure that reduces internal stresses and helps maintain consistent geometry.

## Track Surface

The surface of the track also plays an important role.

If the track is not smooth or if its shape changes slightly along the curve, friction increases and the tambour begins to hesitate during movement.

Careful shaping and finishing of the track are therefore essential parts of the process.

## Tension in the Tambour

Tambours can also bind if internal tension appears during construction.

This may happen if the veneer layout shifts slightly during pressing or if the backing material introduces uneven stress across the surface.

In our workshop the veneering process is carried out in a **vacuum press**, using carefully prepared pressing cauls to keep the structure stable during bonding.

## Movement Should Feel Effortless

When a tambour door works properly, the movement feels natural.

The wooden surface slides into the cabinet almost silently. The mechanism disappears and what remains is simply the movement of the wood.

That moment — when the construction becomes invisible — is one of the most satisfying results of careful furniture making.
