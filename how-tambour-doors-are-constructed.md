# How Tambour Doors Are Constructed

Tambour doors often look deceptively simple. From the outside they appear to be a wooden surface that slides quietly into the cabinet. But from a workshop perspective they are one of the most demanding elements in furniture construction.

In our studio at [Bensari Workshop](https://www.bensariworkshop.com) tambours have become a recurring feature of the furniture I design and build. Over time they have also become one of the recognizable characteristics of the **Bensari Ébénistes** pieces.

Building a tambour door requires careful control of materials, geometry and movement.

## The Structural Base

One important detail that is often misunderstood is the material used to build tambours.

In our workshop tambours are **not made from solid wood slats**. Instead, the structural base is **plywood which is then veneered**.

This approach provides stability that is essential for a moving element. Solid wood tends to move with humidity changes, while plywood offers a stable multilayer structure that keeps the tambour geometry consistent.

## Geometry

Geometry is the most critical aspect of tambour construction.

The radius of the tracks, the spacing of the elements and the alignment of the entire system must work together perfectly. Even very small deviations can affect the smoothness of movement.

Tambours are unforgiving in this respect. If something is even slightly out of alignment, the door will immediately reveal it.

## Tracks

Designing the track is one of the key stages of the process.

The track defines how the tambour moves inside the cabinet. It must be smooth, continuous and precisely integrated with the cabinet structure.

When the geometry is correct, the tambour moves effortlessly. When it is not, the mechanism quickly begins to bind.

## Movement

When everything works together — the plywood structure, the veneer layout and the track geometry — the tambour begins to move almost silently.

At that moment the mechanism disappears and only the movement remains.

For me this is one of the most satisfying moments in furniture making.
