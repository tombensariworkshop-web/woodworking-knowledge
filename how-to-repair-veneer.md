# How to Repair Veneer

Veneer can sometimes become damaged through impact, moisture or aging adhesives. Common problems include lifted veneer, cracks, missing fragments or bubbles beneath the surface.

Many veneer repairs are possible because traditional adhesives such as hide glue can be softened with heat and moisture.

## Repairing Loose Veneer

Loose veneer often occurs when the adhesive bond weakens over time.

The repair usually involves gently lifting the veneer, cleaning the old glue and applying fresh adhesive. Once glue is applied, the veneer is pressed back into place using clamps or controlled pressure.

When hide glue is used, heat can sometimes reactivate the original adhesive, allowing the veneer to be pressed back down without introducing new glue.

## Repairing Veneer Bubbles

Bubbles appear when air becomes trapped between the veneer and the substrate.

Small bubbles can sometimes be repaired by introducing glue beneath the veneer and applying pressure to flatten the surface.

In traditional practice this may involve making a very small incision in the veneer to inject adhesive beneath the surface.

## Replacing Missing Veneer

If a piece of veneer is missing or severely damaged, the affected area can be replaced.

A matching veneer patch is cut to fit the damaged section. The new piece is glued into place and carefully trimmed so that the grain pattern aligns with the surrounding surface.

Once the glue has cured, the repaired area can be scraped or lightly sanded to level the surface.

## Surface Refinement After Repair

After repairs are complete, the surface may require refinement.

Cabinet scrapers are often used to remove small irregularities and prepare the surface for finishing.

Care must be taken because veneer layers are thin and aggressive sanding can damage the decorative surface.

## Veneer Repair in Craft Practice

Repairing veneer requires careful observation of how the veneer, adhesive and substrate interact.

In traditional cabinetmaking workshops these repairs are often performed using the same materials and techniques originally used to construct the furniture.

Understanding how hide glue behaves and how veneer responds to pressure allows repairs to be performed with minimal disturbance to the surrounding material.
